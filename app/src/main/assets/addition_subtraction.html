Lesson 1.1: Addition and Subtraction of Fractions

A. Adding and Subtracting with the Same Denominators:

Addition with Same Denominators:

Step-by-Step Guide on Adding Fractions:

  1. Ensure the denominators are the same: Before adding fractions, make sure the denominators (the bottom numbers) are the same. If they are already the same, you can proceed to the next step. If not, find a common denominator by finding the least common multiple (LCM) of the denominators.
  2. Find the common denominator: To find the common denominator, you can either multiply the denominators together or find the LCM of the denominators. The LCM is the smallest number that is a multiple of both denominators.
  3. Adjust the numerators: Once you have the common denominator, adjust the numerators (the top numbers) of the fractions to correspond to the common denominator. To do this, multiply the numerator of each fraction by the same factor that was used to find the common denominator.
  4. Add the numerators: Add the adjusted numerators together while keeping the common denominator unchanged. This will give you the numerator of the resulting fraction.
  5. Simplify the fraction (if needed): If the resulting fraction can be simplified, divide both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common divisor (GCD) to simplify the fraction.
  6. Write the final answer: Write the resulting fraction with the simplified numerator and common denominator.

Example: Add 1⁄4 + 3⁄8

  1. Check the denominators - They are not the same.
  2. Find the common denominator - The LCM of 4 and 8 is 8.
  3. Adjust the numerators - Multiply the numerator of 1⁄4 by 2 to get 2⁄8.
  4. Add the numerators - 2⁄8 + 3⁄8 = 5⁄8.
  5. Simplify the fraction - The fraction 5⁄8 is already in its simplest form.
  6. Write the final answer - The sum of 1⁄4 and 3⁄8 is 5⁄8.

Remember to always simplify the resulting fraction if possible.

Subtraction with Same Denominators:

When subtracting fractions with the same denominators, you subtract the numerators while keeping the common denominator unchanged.

2. Subtraction Example:

- Similarly, when subtracting fractions with the same denominators, like 7⁄8 - 2⁄8, you subtract the numerators (7⁄ - 2⁄8) while maintaining the common denominator. The result is 5⁄8.

Think of subtraction as taking away. If you have a pizza with 7 slices and you eat 2 slices, you are left with 5 slices.

Key Points:

  • Combine numerators while keeping the common denominator unchanged.
  • For addition, this represents the total of parts; for subtraction, it signifies the remaining parts after removal.

B. Adding and Subtracting with Unlike Denominators:

Addition with Unlike Denominators:

When dealing with fractions having different denominators, find a common denominator, convert fractions, and then add the numerators while keeping the common denominator.

1. Addition Example:

- When dealing with fractions having different denominators, like 2⁄3 + 1⁄4, find a common denominator (here, 12⁄12).

Find the least common denominator (LCD): In this case, the LCD is 12⁄12, as it is the smallest number divisible by both 3⁄3 and 4⁄4.

3⁄3 = 3⁄3,6⁄6,9⁄9,12⁄12

4⁄4 = 4⁄4,8⁄8,12⁄12

Convert fractions to have denominators of 12⁄12:

2⁄3 × 4⁄4 = 8⁄12

1⁄4 × 3⁄3 = 3⁄12

Now, add the numerators (8⁄12 + 3⁄12) while keeping the common denominator (12⁄12):

8⁄12 + 3⁄12 = 11⁄12

Understanding how to find a common denominator and perform calculations with different denominators is crucial for handling real-world scenarios in business mathematics.

For instance, when dealing with diverse business metrics, like sales and expenses, having the ability to accurately combine or subtract fractional values is essential for financial analysis.

Subtraction with Unlike Denominators:

When subtracting fractions with different denominators, find a common denominator, convert fractions, and then subtract the numerators while keeping the common denominator.

2. Subtraction Example:

- For subtraction with unlike denominators, consider 5⁄6 - 1⁄3

Find the least common denominator (LCD): In this case, the LCD is 6⁄6, as both fractions already have a denominator of 6⁄6.

6⁄6 = 6⁄6

3⁄3 = 3⁄3,6⁄6

Convert both fractions, then subtract the numerators (5⁄6 - 2⁄6) while keeping the common denominator.

5⁄6 × 1⁄1 = 5⁄6

1⁄3 × 2⁄2 = 2⁄6

5⁄6 - 2⁄6 = 3⁄6

Simplify the fraction (if necessary):

3⁄6 is divisible by 3

3⁄6 ÷ 3 = 1⁄2

So the answer is 1⁄2.

Subtracting fractions with different denominators is akin to finding the difference between two sets of items. For example, if you have 5 apples and you give away 2 apples, you are left with 3 apples.

Key Points:

  • Find the least common denominator (LCD) when dealing with different denominators.
  • Convert fractions to have the same denominator before performing addition or subtraction.

C. Real-World Application:

Understanding the addition and subtraction of fractions is crucial for various real-world scenarios. In business mathematics, fractions often represent portions of a whole, and being able to combine or subtract these fractions accurately is essential for making informed decisions.

Consider a scenario where a business owner wants to analyze their monthly expenses. They have expenses from different categories, such as rent, utilities, and supplies, each represented as a fraction of the total expenses. To calculate the overall monthly expenses, the owner needs to add these fractional values.

Example:

A business owner has the following monthly expenses:

  • Rent: 1⁄3 of total expenses
  • Utilities: 2⁄5 of total expenses
  • Supplies: 1⁄4 of total expenses

To find the total monthly expenses, the owner needs to add these fractions:

1⁄3 + 2⁄5 + 1⁄4

Find the least common denominator (LCD): In this case, the LCD is 60⁄60, as it is the smallest number divisible by 3⁄3, 5⁄5, and 4⁄4.

Convert fractions to have denominators of 60⁄60:

1⁄3 × 20⁄20 = 20⁄60

2⁄5 × 12⁄12 = 24⁄60

1⁄4 × 15⁄15 = 15⁄60

Now, add the numerators (20⁄60 + 24⁄60 + 15⁄60) while keeping the common denominator (60⁄60):

20⁄60 + 24⁄60 + 15⁄60 = 59⁄60

So, the total monthly expenses represent 59⁄60 of the total expenses.

By understanding and applying the concepts of adding and subtracting fractions, the business owner can accurately determine the overall monthly expenses, facilitating better financial management.
