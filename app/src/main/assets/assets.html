Assets

Assets are properties or rights on properties owned by the business. They are items of value that belong to the business. In general, common examples of assets are cash, tools, equipment, building, and land.

An asset is under the control and custodianship of the entity as a result of a past transaction such as a purchase, an exchange, or a donation. The asset will generate for the entity. The following describe further what an asset is.

  • Assets are controlled by the enterprise.
  • Assets result from past events.
  • Assets give future economic benefits.
  • Assets can be used by themselves alone to yield income.

Different kinds of assets can be used to generate income. A set of kitchen utensils is used to cook the dish that served at a restaurant. Hence, both the kitchen utensils and the restaurant premises (if owned by the business) are assets.

Specifically, assets include cash and cash equivalent, notes receivable, accounts receivable, inventories, prepaid expenses, property, plant and equipment, investment, intangible assets, and other assets.

Tangible Assets and Intangible Assets

Tangible assets have a physical existence that can be touched and felt. They are used primarily in the operation of the business to produce products or services. Examples of tangible assets include buildings, automobiles, physical inventory, furniture, and machines. Tangible assets are depreciated over time as they are used in the business.

Intangible assets, on the other hand, do not have a physical character, but they are essential to the continued operation of a business. Examples of intangible assets include goodwill, intellectual property (patents, copyrights, and trademarks), brand names, customer relationships, contracts, and non-compete agreements.

Current Assets and Non-Current Assets

Current assets are assets that are expected to be converted into cash or used up within one year or less. These assets are used to fund day-to-day operations and current expenditure. Examples of current assets include cash and cash equivalents, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses, and marketable securities.

Non-current assets, on the other hand, are assets that are not expected to be converted into cash or used up within one year. These assets are seen as long-term investments, which are intended to create long-term benefits. Examples of non-current assets include land, property, plant, and equipment (PP&E), long-term investments, trademarks, and goodwill.

Examples of Assets

  • Cash and Cash Equivalents: This includes physical cash, checking accounts, savings accounts, and other highly liquid assets that can be easily converted into cash.
  • Investments: Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and other securities that can be sold for a profit or provide a return on investment.
  • Real Estate: Land, buildings, and other property that can be used for business operations, leased to tenants, or sold for a profit.
  • Vehicles: Cars, trucks, and other vehicles that can be used for transportation, delivery, or other business operations.
  • Equipment: Machinery, tools, and other equipment that can be used to produce goods or provide services.
  • Inventory: Raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods that can be sold to customers.
  • Intellectual Property: Patents, trademarks, copyrights, and other intangible assets that can provide a competitive advantage or generate revenue.
  • Brand Recognition: The value of a company's reputation, image, and customer loyalty, which can translate into higher sales and profits.
  • Goodwill: The value of a company's relationships with customers, suppliers, and other stakeholders, which can contribute to its long-term success.
