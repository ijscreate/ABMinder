Understanding Automated Teller Machines (ATMs)

An automated teller machine (ATM) is an electronic telecommunications device that enables customers of financial institutions to perform various financial transactions without the need for direct interaction with bank staff.

ATMs, known by various names such as automatic teller machines (ATM), automated banking machines (ABM), cashpoints, and cash machines, are accessed using a plastic ATM card with a personal identification number (PIN) for authentication.

The first ATM was installed in London in 1967, and since then, they have become a ubiquitous part of the financial landscape, with millions installed worldwide. However, their use has been gradually declining with the increase in cashless payment systems.

ATMs are connected to interbank networks, allowing customers to withdraw and deposit money from machines not belonging to their bank. However, ATM use may incur fees, charged to non-customers or all users.

ATMs rely on authorization from the card issuer or other authorizing institution, often performed through an ISO 8583 messaging system. They connect directly to their host or ATM Controller using ADSL or dial-up modem over a telephone line or leased lines.

Types of ATMs:

  • Basic Unit ATMs: Allow cash withdrawals, balance inquiries, PIN changes, mini statements, and account updates.
  • Complex Unit ATMs: Provide additional features such as cash or check deposits, line of credit payments, and bill payments.

Categories Based on Labels:

  • Green Label ATMs: Agricultural use
  • Yellow Label ATMs: E-commerce transactions
  • Orange Label ATMs: Share transactions
  • Pink Label ATMs: Dedicated for females to reduce queue times
  • White Label ATMs: Not owned by a specific bank
  • Brown Label ATMs: Operated by third-party entities
  • Biometric ATMs: Require fingerprints or eye scanners for operation

Advantages of Full-Service ATMs:

  • No Upfront Costs: No initial investment for the business owner.
  • No Maintenance Responsibility: Service provider handles repairs and maintenance.
  • Cash Management: Service provider manages cash stocking.
  • Surcharge Revenue: Business owner receives revenue from ATM transactions.
  • Increased Foot Traffic: ATMs attract more customers.
  • Boost in Impulse Purchases: Customers tend to make more purchases with cash in hand.
