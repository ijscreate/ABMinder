Compound Interest

What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is the interest calculated on the initial principal, which also includes all of the accumulated interest from previous periods on a deposit or loan. The concept of compound interest is that interest is added to the principal, creating a larger base on which future interest is calculated. This results in exponential growth over time, as the interest earned in each period becomes part of the principal for the next period.

Calculation of Compound Interest

The formula for calculating compound interest is:

A = P ( 1 + r n ) n t A = P \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}

Where:

  • A is the amount of money accumulated after n years, including interest.
  • P is the principal amount (the initial amount of money).
  • r is the annual interest rate (in decimal).
  • n is the number of times interest is compounded per year.
  • t is the number of years the money is invested or borrowed for.

Compounding Frequencies

Compound interest can be compounded yearly, half-yearly, quarterly, monthly, daily, etc. The frequency of compounding affects the final amount accrued. Here are the corresponding formulas:

  • Annually: Interest is compounded once per year. Here, n = 1.
  • Semi-Annually: Interest is compounded twice per year. Here, n = 2.
  • Quarterly: Interest is compounded four times per year. Here, n = 4.
  • Monthly: Interest is compounded twelve times per year. Here, n = 12.
  • Daily: Interest is compounded every day. Here, n = 365.

Examples of Compound Interest

Annually Compounded Interest:

Let’s say you invest $10,000 in a savings account that earns an annual interest rate of 5% compounded annually. After one year, your investment would grow to:

A = $ 10 , 000 ( 1 + 0.05 1 ) 1 × 1 = $ 10 , 500 A = \$10,000 \left(1 + \frac{0.05}{1}\right)^{1 \times 1} = \$10,500

Semi-Annually Compounded Interest:

Now, let’s say the same savings account compounds interest semi-annually. After one year, your investment would grow to:

A = $ 10 , 000 ( 1 + 0.05 2 ) 2 × 1 = $ 10 , 506.25 A = \$10,000 \left(1 + \frac{0.05}{2}\right)^{2 \times 1} = \$10,506.25

Quarterly Compounded Interest:

If the interest is compounded quarterly, your investment after one year would be:

A = $ 10 , 000 ( 1 + 0.05 4 ) 4 × 1 = $ 10 , 509.41 A = \$10,000 \left(1 + \frac{0.05}{4}\right)^{4 \times 1} = \$10,509.41

Monthly Compounded Interest:

If the interest is compounded monthly, your investment after one year would be:

A = $ 10 , 000 ( 1 + 0.05 12 ) 12 × 1 = $ 10 , 511.62 A = \$10,000 \left(1 + \frac{0.05}{12}\right)^{12 \times 1} = \$10,511.62

Daily Compounded Interest:

If the interest is compounded daily, your investment after one year would be:

A = $ 10 , 000 ( 1 + 0.05 365 ) 365 × 1 = $ 10 , 512.67 A = \$10,000 \left(1 + \frac{0.05}{365}\right)^{365 \times 1} = \$10,512.67

As you can see, the more frequently the interest is compounded, the more your investment grows. However, the difference becomes less significant as the compounding frequency increases.

Let’s consider another example where you deposit $5,000 in a bank account that offers an annual interest rate of 4% compounded monthly for 3 years.

A = $ 5 , 000 ( 1 + 0.04 12 ) 12 × 3 = $ 5 , 624.89 A = \$5,000 \left(1 + \frac{0.04}{12}\right)^{12 \times 3} = \$5,624.89

Amount of Interest Earned: The amount of interest earned is the difference between the final amount and the initial principal:

I n t e r e s t = A − P = $ 5 , 624.89 − $ 5 , 000 = $ 624.89 Interest = A - P = \$5,624.89 - \$5,000 = \$624.89

So, after 3 years, your investment would grow to $5,624.89, and you would have earned $624.89 in interest. This is the power of compound interest! It allows your money to grow faster because interest is calculated on the initial principal and all of the accumulated interest.

Effective Annual Interest Rate

The effective rate of interest, also known as the effective annual interest rate or the annual equivalent rate (AER), is the interest rate that actually gets paid after taking into account the effects of compounding.

The formula for the effective annual interest rate is:

i = ( 1 + n r ) n − 1 i = \left(1 + \frac{n}{r}\right)^{n-1}

Where:

  • i is the effective interest rate
  • r is the nominal interest rate
  • n is the number of compounding periods per year

The effective annual interest rate is a more accurate measure of interest charges than the nominal rate because it takes into account the frequency of compounding. The more frequent the compounding periods, the higher the effective interest rate.

For example, if you have a nominal interest rate of 10% compounded monthly, the effective annual interest rate would be:

i = ( 1 + 0.10 12 ) 12 − 1 = 0.1047 = 10.47 % i = \left(1 + \frac{0.10}{12}\right)^{12} - 1 = 0.1047 = 10.47\%

This means that even though the stated interest rate is 10%, the interest you actually pay or earn in a year is 10.47% due to monthly compounding.

Pros and Cons of Compound Interest

Pros:

  • Growth of Investments: Compound interest can help investors grow their money. It multiplies money at an accelerated rate, making your capital grow reasonably quickly.
  • Beneficial for Long-Term Investments: Understanding the pros and cons of compound interest can be beneficial to those with long-term investments.
  • Better Comparisons and Judgements: If you understand compound interest, you can make better comparisons and judgements. For example, some mortgages compound every day, some every year – which would you choose?

Cons:

  • Cost Can Be Disguised: The cost is disguised and can run away with your money. Missing a payment by a day may mean interest falls due to be calculated before the payment is recorded.
  • Designed to Help Lenders: Compound interest is designed to help lenders. Credit card monthly repayments are usually set so you are encouraged to keep borrowing and thus keep paying interest.
  • Can Make Debt More Difficult: Compound interest can make debt more difficult. It’s the effect of ‘interest on interest’ and happens when interest is added to the capital or principal sum so that from that moment on, the interest which has been added also itself earns interest.

Key Points:

  • Compound interest is interest calculated on the initial principal, which also includes all of the accumulated interest from previous periods.
  • The formula for compound interest takes into account the principal amount, interest rate, and the number of time periods the money is invested or borrowed for.
  • Compound interest leads to exponential growth of funds over time, as interest is continuously added to the principal amount.
  • Compounding frequency (e.g., annually, semi-annually, monthly) affects the final amount accrued.
  • Compound interest is widely used in savings accounts, investments, loans, and mortgages.
  • It is important to understand compound interest when making financial decisions to maximize returns or minimize costs.
