Cooperatives

A cooperative is a business organization owned and operated by a group of individuals for their mutual benefit. It operates on the principle of cooperation and aims to meet the common economic, social, and cultural needs of its members. Cooperatives are democratically controlled and member-owned, with each member having an equal say in the organization's decision-making processes.

Characteristics of Cooperatives

  • Voluntary Membership: Membership in a cooperative is voluntary and open to all individuals, regardless of their social, political, racial, or religious backgrounds and beliefs.
  • Common Bond of Interest: Individuals in a cooperative share a common bond of interest and join together to achieve a common social or economic goal.
  • Equitable Contributions: Members of a cooperative make equitable contributions to the capital required for the cooperative and accept a fair share of the risks and benefits of the undertaking.
  • Primary Objective: The primary objective of a cooperative is to provide goods and services to its members and enable them to attain increased income and savings.
  • Formation: A cooperative may be formed by at least 15 persons for various purposes, including encouraging thrift and savings, generating funds and extending credit, promoting systematic production and marketing, providing goods and services to members, developing expertise and skills, acquiring lands and providing housing benefits, insuring against losses, and promoting economic, social, and educational advancement.
  • Legal Entity: A cooperative can sue and be sued under its name and has the right to succession.
  • Limited Liability: Members of a cooperative are subject to limited liability.
  • Duration: A cooperative shall exist for a period not exceeding 50 years from the date of formation, with the possibility of extension for additional periods not exceeding 50 years.
  • Board of Directors: A cooperative has its set of board of directors.
  • Net Surplus: Income of a cooperative, called net surplus, belongs to its members.

Types of Cooperatives

  1. Consumer and Purchasing Cooperatives

    Consumer and purchasing cooperatives are businesses owned and managed by their customers. These cooperatives aim to provide goods and services to their members. The consumers of the products or services offered by the cooperative are also the providers of capital used to finance the entity. These cooperatives may be in various industries, such as retailing, grocery, healthcare, and utilities. A purchasing cooperative for retail members is a way of sharing marketing expenses and using their economy of scale to negotiate concessions and discounts from manufacturers.

  2. Financial Cooperatives

    Financial cooperatives provide financial and insurance services to their members. Credit unions are popular financial cooperatives owned and managed by members with a common need for financial services, such as deposits and loans.   3. Producer and Processing Cooperatives

    Producers and processing co-ops produce, process, and market the goods and services of their members. They aim to realize economies of scale by gathering together to market and supply value-added products and services. Many are in the agricultural industry, while others are in the creative and artisan sectors.

  4. Housing Cooperatives

    Housing cooperatives own real estate properties comprising one or more residential buildings. Unlike other kinds of real estate ownership, such as condominiums, housing cooperatives are member-based. Each member has the right to occupy one housing unit in a property developed by the cooperative.

  5. Worker Cooperatives

    Worker cooperatives exist to provide job opportunities and employment for their members. Many small and medium enterprises operate as cooperatives in various industries, including food, construction, manufacturing, education, and home care industries. Employees participate in profit sharing via their ownership of the company.

Advantages of Cooperatives

  • Democratic Control: Members have an equal say in the decision-making processes of the cooperative, regardless of their financial contributions.
  • Shared Benefits: Profits generated by the cooperative are distributed among members in proportion to their participation, rather than being retained by external shareholders.
  • Community Development: Cooperatives contribute to the economic development of their communities by providing employment opportunities, affordable goods and services, and support for local producers.
  • Stability: Cooperatives tend to be more resilient in times of economic downturns, as they prioritize the long-term interests of their members over short-term profits.

Disadvantages of Cooperatives

  • Decision-Making Challenges: Achieving consensus among members can be time-consuming and challenging, particularly in larger cooperatives with diverse membership.
  • Capital Constraints: Cooperatives may face difficulties raising capital from external sources, as investors may be reluctant to invest in organizations with shared ownership and limited profit potential.
  • Management Issues: Ensuring effective management and leadership within the cooperative can be challenging, particularly in worker cooperatives where members may lack business management experience.
  • Competitive Disadvantages: Cooperatives may face challenges competing with traditional business models that prioritize profit maximization and shareholder value.
