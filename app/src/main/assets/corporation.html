Corporation

A corporation is a legal entity that is separate and distinct from its owners. Corporations enjoy most of the rights and responsibilities that individuals possess: they can enter contracts, loan and borrow money, sue and be sued, hire employees, own assets, and pay taxes. Some refer to it as a "legal person."

Characteristics of a Corporation

  • Legal entity: A corporation is a separate legal entity from its owners, meaning it can enter into contracts, sue, and be sued in its own name.
  • Limited liability: Shareholders are typically not personally liable for the debts and obligations of the corporation. Their liability is limited to the amount of their investment.
  • Perpetual existence: A corporation has a continuous existence, even if its owners or shareholders change. Its existence is not dependent on the lives of its owners.
  • Centralized management: Corporations are managed by a board of directors elected by shareholders. Day-to-day operations are typically overseen by officers appointed by the board.
  • Transferable ownership: Ownership interests in a corporation, typically in the form of stock, can be easily transferred between shareholders.

Advantages of a Corporation

  • Limited Liability: Shareholders of a corporation have limited liability, meaning they are not personally responsible for the debts and obligations of the corporation. This means that their personal assets are protected in the event of bankruptcy or legal issues.
  • Access to Capital: Corporations can raise capital through the sale of stocks and bonds, providing them with access to larger amounts of capital than other business structures.
  • Perpetual Existence: Corporations have perpetual existence, meaning they continue to exist even if the ownership changes. This provides stability and continuity to the business.
  • Ability to attract and retain employees through stock options and other incentives
  • Transferability of Ownership: Ownership in a corporation can be easily transferred through the sale of stocks, providing flexibility to shareholders who want to sell their ownership or to new investors who want to buy in.
  • Tax Benefits: Corporations may enjoy tax benefits, such as the ability to deduct business expenses and the lower tax rate on corporate income.

Disadvantages of a Corporation

  • Double Taxation: Corporations are subject to double taxation, meaning the corporation is taxed on its income, and shareholders are taxed on their dividends.
  • Cost and Complexity: Corporations are more costly and complex to set up and maintain than other business structures, requiring more legal and accounting services.
  • Government Regulation: Corporations are subject to more government regulation and oversight than other business structures, including securities regulations and reporting requirements.
  • Shareholder Expectations: Corporations have a fiduciary responsibility to act in the best interest of the shareholders, which can sometimes conflict with the interests of other stakeholders, such as employees or the community.
  • Lack of Privacy: Corporations are required to disclose more information to the public than other business structures, reducing privacy for the owners.

Example:

Jollibee Foods Corporation (JFC) is a well-known corporation in the Philippines. It is the largest fast-food chain in the country and one of the largest in Asia, with over 3,200 stores worldwide. JFC was established in 1975 as a family-owned business and was incorporated as a corporation in 1978.

As a corporation, JFC has a separate legal personality from its shareholders, providing limited liability protection to its shareholders. This means that the shareholders are not personally liable for the debts and obligations of the corporation, and their personal assets are protected in the event of bankruptcy or legal issues.

JFC has access to a large amount of capital through the sale of stocks and bonds, allowing it to expand its operations and open new stores both domestically and internationally. The corporation's ownership is easily transferable through the sale of stocks, providing flexibility to shareholders who want to sell their ownership or to new investors who want to buy in.

JFC is subject to double taxation, meaning the corporation is taxed on its income, and shareholders are taxed on their dividends. However, the corporation enjoys tax benefits, such as the ability to deduct business expenses and the lower tax rate on corporate income.

JFC is also subject to government regulation and oversight, including securities regulations and reporting requirements. As a public company, JFC is required to disclose more information to the public than other business structures, reducing privacy for the owners. However, this transparency can also build trust with stakeholders, such as investors and customers.

Overall, JFC's status as a corporation has allowed it to grow and expand its operations, providing jobs and contributing to the Philippine economy. It has also allowed the company to access large amounts of capital, enjoy tax benefits, and provide limited liability protection to its shareholders. However, the corporation is also subject to double taxation, government regulation, and reduced privacy for the owners.
