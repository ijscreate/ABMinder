Lesson 1.3: Decimals and their Operations

A. Introduction to Decimals:

What are Decimals?

Decimals are a way of expressing parts of a whole. They are based on powers of 10 and are represented by a decimal point.

Example: 0.25 represents 25 hundredths or 1/4.

Key Points:

  • Decimals are an extension of the whole number system.
  • The decimal point separates the whole number part from the fractional part.
  • Decimal fractions are based on powers of 10 (tenths, hundredths, thousandths, etc.).

B. Decimal Operations:

Basic Decimal Operations:

Decimal operations include addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. These operations are similar to whole numbers with attention to the placement of the decimal point.

Example: Add 0.75 and 0.2

0.75 + 0.2 = 0.95

When adding or subtracting decimals, align the decimal points and perform the operation as if working with whole numbers. The result should have the same number of decimal places as the numbers being added/subtracted.

Example: Multiply 2.5 by 0.4

2.5 × 0.4 = 1.0

When multiplying decimals, ignore the decimal point, multiply as if they were whole numbers, and then place the decimal point in the result. The decimal places in the result should be the sum of the decimal places in the numbers being multiplied.

Example: Divide 3.6 by 1.2

3.6 ÷ 1.2 = 3.0

When dividing decimals, move the decimal point in both the dividend and divisor to make the divisor a whole number. Divide as if they were whole numbers, then place the decimal point in the result. The number of decimal places in the result should be the same as in the dividend.

Key Points:

  • Align decimal points when adding or subtracting decimals.
  • Multiply decimals as if they were whole numbers and place the decimal point in the result.
  • Divide decimals by moving the decimal point in both the dividend and divisor.

C. Types of Decimals:

1. Terminating Decimals:

Terminating decimals are decimals that have a finite number of digits after the decimal point. They can be expressed as exact fractions.

Example: 0.25, 1.5

2. Repeating Decimals:

Repeating decimals are decimals that have a repeating pattern of digits after the decimal point. They can be expressed as fractions with repeating patterns.

Example: 0.333..., 1.2727...

3. Non-terminating Non-repeating Decimals:

Non-terminating non-repeating decimals are decimals that neither terminate nor have a repeating pattern. They go on indefinitely without a specific sequence.

Example: √2 (the square root of 2)

D. Place Values of Decimals:

Understanding Place Values:

The place value of a digit in a decimal is determined by its position from the decimal point. The common place values include tenths, hundredths, thousandths, and so on.

For instance, consider the decimal 12.345:

  • The digit 5 is in the thousandths place.
  • The digit 4 is in the hundredths place.
  • The digit 3 is in the tenths place.
  • The digits 2 are in the ones place.

    • Example: In the decimal 12.345, the digit 4 is in the hundredths place.

    Key Place Values:

  • Trillions: Digits to the left of the decimal point represent trillions.
  • Billions: The first digit to the left of the decimal point represents billions.
  • Millions: The second digit to the left of the decimal point represents millions.
  • Thousands: The third digit to the left of the decimal point represents thousands.
  • Ones: Digits to the left of the decimal point represent whole numbers.
  • Tenths: The first digit to the right of the decimal point.
  • Hundredths: The second digit to the right of the decimal point.
  • Thousandths: The third digit to the right of the decimal point.
  • Ten Thousandths: The fourth digit to the right of the decimal point.
  • Hundred Thousandths: The fifth digit to the right of the decimal point.
  • Millionths: The sixth digit to the right of the decimal point.

    • E. Real-World Application:

    Understanding decimal operations, types of decimals, and place values is crucial for various real-world applications. In financial contexts, decimals are often used to represent percentages, prices, and quantities.

    Example:

    A store is offering a 15% discount on a product priced at $45. Calculate the discounted price.

    Original Price: $45

    Discount: 15%

    Discounted Price: $45 - (15% of $45)

    $45 - (0.15 × $45) = $38.25

    The discounted price of the product is $38.25.

    By applying decimal operations, businesses can make informed decisions regarding pricing, discounts, and financial planning.
