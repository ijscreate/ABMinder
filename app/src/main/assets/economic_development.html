Economic Development

Economic development is the process of improving the economic well-being and quality of life for a community. It involves programs, policies, or activities that seek to enhance the economic well-being and quality of life for a community, with the aim of creating wealth and community benefits.

Economic development can be achieved through various approaches, including business retention and expansion, attracting new businesses, and encouraging the growth of new businesses. The goal is to create sustained economic growth that provides sufficient incomes for the local labor force, profitable business opportunities for employers, and tax revenues for maintaining an infrastructure to support this continued growth.

Economic development is not the same as community development, which is a process for making a community a better place to live and work. Instead, economic development is purely and simply the creation of wealth in which community benefits are created.

Economic development is crucial for underdeveloped nations, as understanding the theory of economic development is essential for their evolution into sophisticated and relatively self-sufficient economies. Despite the widespread use of the term, there is no universally accepted definition of a developing country or what constitutes the process of economic development. Developing countries are typically categorized based on a per capita income criterion, with economic development being thought to occur as per capita incomes rise.

There are several challenges associated with measuring both the level of per capita income and its rate of growth, including borderline cases, technical difficulties in converting national incomes into a common denominator, and estimating the value of noncash components of real incomes in underdeveloped countries. Despite these challenges, measures of per capita income correlate reasonably well with other measures of economic well-being, such as life expectancy, infant mortality rates, and literacy rates.

Economic development is crucial for job creation and improving the material standards of living for a community. Economic development involves qualitative and quantitative improvements in a country's economy, including job creation and raising per capita incomes. Creating more and better quality jobs is key to boosting growth, reducing poverty, and increasing social cohesion.

Empirical studies have found that economic growth tends to be positively associated with job creation. For example, Khan (2007) finds that employment elasticity of GDP growth in developing countries is 0.7, meaning that a 1% increase in GDP growth leads to a 0.7% increase in employment. Kapsos (2005) finds that for every 1-percentage point of additional GDP growth, total employment has grown between 0.3 and 0.38 percentage points during the three periods between 1991 and 2003.

However, it is important that growth occurs in sectors that have the potential to absorb labor at a large scale. Some sectors and activities are more employment-intensive than others. For example, a literature review by Basnett and Sen (2013) identifies an extensive body of evidence which suggests that growth in manufacturing and services have a particularly positive impact on employment. The impact of GDP growth on employment in agriculture is found to be limited overall, while value-added growth in the agriculture sector has a relatively large impact on employment. For textiles, the body of evidence was small, but the studies suggest that growth positively contributed to job creation. For agri-business/food processing, the authors find a positive impact of growth on employment. Melamed, Hartwig and Grant (2011) suggest that growth in services is becoming relatively more important in driving employment than manufacturing.
