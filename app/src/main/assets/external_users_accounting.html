Overview of External Users of Accounting Information

Customers

Customers are interested in a company's accounting information to determine if it will be profitable for them to transact with the business. For example, a customer might review a company's financial statements to assess the company's profitability, product quality, reliability, and future commitments before deciding to make a purchase.

Creditors

Creditors use a company's financial statements to assess the risk associated with lending to the company. They consider factors such as the profitability of the company, the amount of borrowings, and the riskiness of lending. For instance, a creditor may analyze a company's financial ratios to determine its ability to repay loans and the likelihood of default.

Investors

Investors use a company's financial statements to decide if they will invest in the business. They seek companies that can provide a decent return on their investment and evaluate the company's financial ratios to get a feel for how the company operates. Investors also look out for the earnings retention policy of a company, as this information is a key indicator if an investment will be profitable.

Government

The government uses a company's financial statements for regulatory purposes, such as ensuring compliance with tax laws and guidelines. They also use financial statements to compute the amount of taxes payable by a company and to scrutinize businesses, especially large ones, to prevent financial collapse.

Academe

Members of the academe, such as professors, researchers, and students, benefit from accounting information in a company's financial statements for academic purposes. They may use financial statements to study particular trends in a specific industry or the economy as a whole, identify loopholes and possible improvements in the accounting standards, and improve the accountancy practice in the country.

General Public

The general public is also considered an external user of accounting information. They may analyze financial statements to get an idea of how the industry operates and respond to various economic cycles. For example, the public may use financial statements to make decisions about their levels of consumption and spending, whether or not to start a business given the current economic conditions, to stay on their current job or look for a higher-paying job, and the best use of their resources.
