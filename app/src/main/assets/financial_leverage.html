Financial Leverage

What is Financial Leverage?

Financial leverage is the use of borrowed money (debt) to finance the purchase of assets with the expectation that the income or capital gain from the new asset will exceed the cost of borrowing.

In most cases, the provider of the debt will put a limit on how much risk it is ready to take and indicate a limit on the extent of the leverage it will allow. In the case of asset-backed lending, the financial provider uses the assets as collateral until the borrower repays the loan. In the case of a cash flow loan, the general creditworthiness of the company is used to back the loan.

This guide will outline how financial leverage works, how it’s measured, and the risks associated with using it.

Let us illustrate how financial leverage works:

When purchasing assets, three options are available to the company for financing: using equity, debt, and leases. Apart from equity, the rest of the options incur fixed costs that are lower than the income that the company expects to earn from the asset. In this case, we assume that the company uses debt to finance asset acquisition.

EXAMPLE 1

Imagine you want to purchase a property worth 1,000,000 pesos. You have two options:

  1. Using your own funds entirely.
  2. Using a combination of your own funds (equity) and borrowed funds (debt).

If you use only your own funds, you invest ₱1,000,000. If the property value increases to ₱1,200,000, your return is ₱200,000 (20% return on investment).

However, if you decide to use financial leverage and invest ₱500,000 of your own money and borrow the remaining ₱500,000, you can invest in two properties worth ₱1,000,000 each.

If the property value increases to ₱1,200,000 each, your return will be ₱400,000 (40% return on your initial investment of ₱1,000,000), doubling your return compared to using your own funds entirely.

LET US DEFINE!

Debt Ratio

The debt ratio measures the proportion of a company's assets that are financed by debt. It is calculated by dividing total debt by total assets.

Debt Ratio = Total Debt Total Assets \text{Debt Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Debt}}{\text{Total Assets}}

Debt-to-Equity Ratio

The debt-to-equity ratio compares a company's total liabilities to its shareholders' equity. It indicates the proportion of equity and debt used by the company to finance its assets.

Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Liabilities Shareholders’ Equity \text{Debt-to-Equity Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Liabilities}}{\text{Shareholders' Equity}}

Interest Coverage Ratio

The interest coverage ratio measures a company's ability to meet its interest payments on outstanding debt. It shows how many times a company can cover its interest expenses with its earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT).

Interest Coverage Ratio = Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT) Interest Expenses \text{Interest Coverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT)}}{\text{Interest Expenses}}

A similar term for interest coverage ratio is “Times Interest Earned (TIE) Ratio.”

Financial leverage can amplify returns, but it also increases risk. Understanding key ratios like the debt ratio, debt-to-equity ratio, and interest coverage ratio is crucial for assessing a company's financial health and its ability to manage debt effectively.

Risks of Financial Leverage

Financial leverage, while potentially increasing a company's earnings, may also result in disproportionate losses.

These losses can occur when the interest expenses on the asset exceed the borrower's returns, such as when the asset's value declines or interest rates rise.

Stock Price Volatility

Excessive financial leverage can lead to significant fluctuations in company profits, resulting in frequent rises and falls in stock prices.

This volatility can make it difficult to accurately account for stock options owned by the company's employees and lead to higher interest payments to shareholders as stock prices increase.

Bankruptcy

In industries with low barriers to entry, revenue and profit fluctuations are more likely, making it easier for companies to fall into bankruptcy as they struggle to meet rising debt obligations and operating expenses.

When a company is unable to pay its debts, creditors may file for bankruptcy in order to auction off the company's assets and recoup their losses.

Reduced Access to Additional Debt

Lenders assess a company's financial leverage when considering new loans.

Companies with high debt-to-equity ratios may have difficulty securing additional funds due to the increased risk of default.

If a lender does agree to extend credit to a highly-leveraged company, it will likely do so at a higher interest rate to compensate for the added risk.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Financial Leverage

Financial leverage refers to the use of debt to finance a company's assets, with the expectation that the returns from the assets will exceed the cost of borrowing. Here are some advantages and disadvantages of financial leverage:

Advantages:

  • Increased returns: Financial leverage can amplify a company's returns, allowing it to earn higher profits with the same amount of assets.
  • Tax benefits: Interest payments on debt are tax-deductible, reducing a company's taxable income and lowering its overall tax burden.
  • Flexibility: Debt financing can provide a company with more flexibility than equity financing, as it allows the company to maintain control over its operations and decision-making.

Disadvantages:

  • Increased risk: Financial leverage can also amplify a company's losses, potentially leading to bankruptcy if the returns from the assets are not sufficient to cover the interest expenses.
  • Higher fixed costs: Interest payments on debt are fixed costs, meaning they must be paid regardless of the company's profitability. This can put a strain on a company's cash flow, particularly in times of economic downturn.
  • Reduced access to debt: Companies with high levels of financial leverage may have difficulty securing additional debt financing, as lenders may view them as too risky.
  • Reduced financial flexibility: High levels of financial leverage can limit a company's financial flexibility, making it more difficult to respond to changes in the business environment.
