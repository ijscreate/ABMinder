Fixing

What Is Fixing?

Fixing is the practice of setting the price of a product rather than allowing it to be determined by free-market forces. It becomes illegal when involving collusion among two or more producers or suppliers.

Understanding Fixing

In a free market, the price of a product or service is determined by the law of supply and demand. Fixing disrupts this natural equilibrium and is often a way to force consumers to pay more than they're willing to pay. It usually involves competitors secretly agreeing to keep their prices at a certain level to avoid price competition.

Price-Fixing and Antitrust Laws

Legal implications surrounding price-fixing are governed by antitrust laws like the Sherman Act, aiming to preserve fair competition and protect consumers. Engaging in price-fixing practices can lead to severe legal consequences for companies and individuals involved.

Economic Equilibrium

Price fixing disrupts the natural equilibrium between supply and demand in a competitive market. It artificially sets prices at a level different from what would emerge through free-market forces, leading to a mismatch between supply and demand.

Price-Fixing and Small Businesses

Price fixing can have detrimental effects on small businesses and entrepreneurs, making it challenging for them to maintain competitive pricing or attract customers. The anticompetitive practices may discourage innovation and new market entries.

Horizontal vs. Vertical Fixing

Horizontal price fixing occurs when competitors at the same level of the supply chain, such as rival companies or manufacturers, enter into agreements to set and maintain prices. Vertical price fixing involves agreements between entities at different levels of the supply chain, typically between manufacturers and retailers or distributors.

Reasonable Cases of Price-Fixing

In certain contexts, fixed prices may be reasonable, such as government oversight of essential services, internal collaboration of business groups, legal exemptions for professional associations, price floors, and customized pricing agreements. Each situation may vary and specific circumstances may or may not make each case illegal.

Examples of Price-Fixing

One classic example of price-fixing was carried out in the 1970s by the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries (OAPEC). Another notorious case involved the Swiss pharmaceutical giant Roche in 1999, agreeing to pay a record U.S. fine related to the price of vitamins. Additionally, some countries may peg their currencies to the U.S. dollar, which is a legal form of exchange rate fixing.

Are There Any Legal Exceptions to Price-Fixing Rules?

While there are limited legal exceptions, such as government-regulated price controls in certain industries, most price-fixing agreements are illegal. Businesses should generally avoid engaging in any form of anticompetitive pricing.

How Do Authorities Detect and Investigate Price-Fixing Schemes?

Authorities employ various methods, including whistleblower reports, market monitoring, and data analysis, to detect and investigate price-fixing schemes. Entities can ask internal staff to provide information (without penalty) for information as well.

Can Collaborations Within the Same Corporate Group Involve Price-Fixing?

Collaborations within the same corporate group may involve internal agreements on pricing strategies, which are generally legal. However, these collaborations should not harm external competition.

Are There Instances Where Governments Legally Fix Prices?

Governments may legally fix prices in specific industries, such as utilities. These instances are carefully regulated and monitored to prevent abuse and protect consumer interests. In general, these are situations where the government may want to specifically control an industry and not encourage private companies to enter the market space.

The Bottom Line

Price fixing involves agreements among competitors to set and maintain specific prices for goods or services, restraining healthy market competition. This anticompetitive practice is illegal, violating antitrust laws, and can result in severe legal consequences.
