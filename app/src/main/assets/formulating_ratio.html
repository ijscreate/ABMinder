Lesson 2.1: Formulating and Solving Problems involving Ratios

Understanding Ratios in Problem Solving

Ratios play a crucial role in solving real-world problems by providing a means to compare quantities and establish relationships. When formulating and solving problems involving ratios, it's essential to grasp the context and interpret the ratio in the given scenario.

Let's explore how to formulate and solve problems using ratios through examples and key concepts.

Identifying the Given Ratios

The first step is to identify the ratios provided in the problem. Whether it's a ratio of quantities, distances, or amounts, clearly define the relationship between the numbers involved.

Expressing Ratios in Different Forms

Understand the flexibility of expressing ratios. Whether presented as a:b, a to b, or a/b, these forms represent the same relationship. Choose the format that aligns with the problem's context.

Formulating the Problem

Translate the given information into a mathematical problem. Use the identified ratios to set up the problem statement. For example, if the problem involves a ratio of distances, consider the units of measurement to ensure consistency.

Solving the Problem

Apply the appropriate mathematical operations to find the solution. Depending on the problem, you may need to multiply, divide, or use other operations to determine the desired outcome. Always check the units and ensure the final answer makes sense in the given context.

Example: Applying Ratios to Solve a Real-World Problem

1. Suppose a recipe calls for a ratio of 2 cups of flour to 3 cups of sugar. If you want to double the recipe, how much flour should you use?

Solution:

Identify the given ratio: 2:3

Since you want to double the recipe, multiply both parts of the ratio by 2:

Flour = 2 x 2 = 4 cups

Sugar = 3 x 2 = 6 cups

Therefore, you should use 4 cups of flour to maintain the ratio when doubling the recipe.

2. Imagine you are an architect working on a model of a building. The scaled model needs to represent the actual building accurately. The blueprint specifies a ratio of 1:100 for the model's dimensions to the real dimensions.

Problem: If the height of a specific section in the actual building is 20 meters, how tall should the corresponding section be in the scaled model?

Solution:

Identify the given ratio: 1:100

Apply the ratio to find the height of the scaled model:

Height in Model = 1 x 20 meters / 100 = 0.2 meters

Therefore, the height of the corresponding section in the scaled model should be 0.2 meters to maintain the 1:100 ratio.

3. Consider a chef preparing a salad dressing with a specific oil to vinegar ratio. The original recipe specifies a ratio of 2:1 (2 parts oil to 1 part vinegar).

Problem: If the chef wants to make 500 milliliters of dressing, how much oil and vinegar should be used?

Solution:

Identify the given ratio: 2:1

Calculate the amounts of oil and vinegar based on the ratio and desired quantity:

Oil = 2 parts / (2 + 1) * 500 ml = 333.33 ml

Vinegar = 1 part / (2 + 1) * 500 ml = 166.67 ml

Therefore, to maintain the 2:1 ratio, the chef should use 333.33 ml of oil and 166.67 ml of vinegar to make 500 ml of dressing.

4. Imagine an artist mixing paint colors to achieve a certain shade. The desired ratio is 3 parts blue paint to 2 parts yellow paint.

Problem: If the artist needs 300 milliliters of the mixed color, how much blue and yellow paint should be used?

Solution:

Identify the given ratio: 3:2

Calculate the amounts of blue and yellow paint based on the ratio and desired quantity:

Blue Paint = 3 parts / (3 + 2) * 300 ml = 180 ml

Yellow Paint = 2 parts / (3 + 2) * 300 ml = 120 ml

Therefore, to achieve the desired color, the artist should mix 180 ml of blue paint and 120 ml of yellow paint.

5. Consider a classroom where the teacher wants to maintain a ratio of 4 students with laptops to 3 students without laptops.

Problem: If there are 28 students in the class, how many should have laptops, and how many should not?

Solution:

Identify the given ratio: 4:3

Calculate the number of students with and without laptops based on the ratio and total number of students:

Students with Laptops = 4 parts / (4 + 3) * 28 = 16 students

Students without Laptops = 3 parts / (4 + 3) * 28 = 12 students

Therefore, to maintain the desired ratio, the teacher should have 16 students with laptops and 12 students without laptops in the classroom.

Key Considerations in Ratio Problem Solving

When dealing with ratio problems, it's essential to keep the following key points in mind:

  • Context Matters: Understand the context of the problem to interpret the ratio correctly.
  • Units and Consistency: Pay attention to units and ensure consistency in measurements when working with ratios.
  • Check Your Answer: Verify the final solution to ensure it aligns with the problem statement and makes practical sense.
