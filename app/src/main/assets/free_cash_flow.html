Free Cash Flow

Free cash flow (FCF) is a measure of a company's financial performance and is used to assess its ability to generate cash beyond what is required to maintain its current operations and investments. FCF represents the cash that a company has available to pay dividends, repay debt, make investments, and fund other activities that are not included in its day-to-day operations.

Formula:

FCF = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures

Operating cash flow is the cash a company generates from its core business operations, and it can be found on a company's cash flow statement. Capital expenditures, on the other hand, are the investments a company makes in long-term assets such as property, plant, and equipment.

Example:

Let's say a company has an operating cash flow of ₱20 million and capital expenditures of ₱5 million. To calculate the free cash flow, we would subtract the capital expenditures from the operating cash flow:

FCF = ₱20 million - ₱5 million = ₱15 million

This means that the company has ₱15 million in free cash flow, which it can use to pay dividends, repay debt, invest in new projects, and fund other activities.

Importance:

Free cash flow is a more accurate measure of a company's financial health, as it takes into account the cash generated by the company's operations and the investments it makes in long-term assets. Investors often use free cash flow to assess a company's potential for future growth, as it provides insights into its ability to invest in new products, enter new markets, and make strategic acquisitions.

Limitations:

  • FCF does not account for the quality of cash flow.
  • Using FCF as a performance measure can cause systematic underinvestment.
  • FCF yield may send short-term signals that run counter to maximizing the long-run value of a company.
  • FCF does not consider the impact of non-operating items on a company's cash flow.
