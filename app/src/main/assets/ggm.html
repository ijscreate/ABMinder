Gordon Growth Model: Understanding the Concept

The Gordon Growth Model (GGM) is a financial model used to estimate the intrinsic value of a stock based on its expected future dividends and growth rate. The model is named after its creator, Myron Gordon, who first introduced it in 1962. The GGM is a popular tool among investors and financial analysts because it is simple, easy to use, and provides a quick estimate of a stock's intrinsic value.

The model is based on the concept of the present value of future cash flows. It assumes that the company pays a constant dividend that grows at a constant rate, and that the required rate of return on the stock is constant over time. The model also assumes that the stock is perpetual, meaning it has no maturity date, and that the growth rate of dividends is less than the required rate of return.

The GGM is particularly useful for estimating the intrinsic value of stocks that have a stable and predictable dividend stream, such as utility companies or consumer goods companies. It is less useful for estimating the intrinsic value of stocks that have a volatile dividend stream or no dividend at all, such as technology companies or startups.

Despite its limitations, the Gordon Growth Model is a valuable tool for investors and financial analysts. It provides a framework for thinking about the relationship between a stock's dividend stream, growth rate, and required rate of return. By estimating the intrinsic value of a stock using the GGM, investors can make informed decisions about whether to buy, sell, or hold a particular stock.

Formula

The formula for the Gordon Growth Model is:

Intrinsic Value = D1 / (r - g)

Where:

  • D1: The expected dividend per share for the next year
  • r: The required rate of return on the stock, also known as the discount rate
  • g: The expected growth rate of dividends, assumed to be constant over time

Assumptions

  • The company pays a constant dividend that grows at a constant rate
  • The required rate of return on the stock is constant over time
  • The stock is perpetual, meaning it has no maturity date
  • The growth rate of dividends is less than the required rate of return

Limitations

  • The model assumes a constant growth rate, which may not be realistic for many companies
  • The model assumes a constant required rate of return, which may not be realistic for many investors
  • The model assumes that the company will pay dividends indefinitely, which may not be the case for all companies
  • The model does not take into account changes in the company's capital structure or other factors that may affect the stock's value

Example

Suppose a company has an expected dividend per share of $2.00 for the next year, a required rate of return on the stock of 10%, and an expected growth rate of dividends of 5%. Using the Gordon Growth Model, we can calculate the intrinsic value of the stock as follows:

Intrinsic Value = $2.00 / (0.10 - 0.05) = $40.00

This means that the stock is undervalued if its current market price is below $40.00 and overvalued if its current market price is above $40.00.

Conclusion

The Gordon Growth Model is a useful tool for estimating the intrinsic value of a stock based on its expected future dividends and growth rate. While the model has several limitations, it is simple, easy to use, and provides a quick estimate of a stock's intrinsic value. Investors and financial analysts can use the GGM as part of their investment analysis and decision-making process.
