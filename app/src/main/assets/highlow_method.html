The High-Low Method: Understanding the Concept

The High-Low Method is a simple approach to cost behavior analysis that is used to separate fixed and variable costs in a cost incurred over a range of activity.

The method involves identifying the highest and lowest levels of activity during a given period and calculating the total cost at each level. The difference between the total cost at the high level and the total cost at the low level is attributed to variable costs, while the total cost at the midpoint of the range is attributed to fixed costs.

The High-Low Method is a simple and easy-to-use tool for cost analysis, but it has some limitations. For example, it is based on only two data points, which may not accurately reflect the cost behavior over a wider range of activity. Additionally, it assumes that all variable costs change at the same rate, which may not be the case in reality.

Formula

The formula for the High-Low Method is:

Fixed Cost = Total Cost at Midpoint - (Variable Cost per Unit x Midpoint Activity Level)

Where:

  • Fixed Cost: The cost that remains constant regardless of the level of activity
  • Total Cost at Midpoint: The total cost at the midpoint of the range of activity
  • Variable Cost per Unit: The cost that varies with the level of activity
  • Midpoint Activity Level: The activity level at the midpoint of the range of activity

Example

Suppose a company incurs the following costs over a range of production levels:

Production Level Total Cost
1,000 units $10,000
2,000 units $15,000
3,000 units $20,000
4,000 units $25,000

Using the High-Low Method, we can calculate the fixed and variable costs as follows:

  • Variable Cost per Unit = ($15,000 - $10,000) / (2,000 - 1,000) = $5.00
  • Fixed Cost = $20,000 - ($5.00 x 2,500) = $10,000

Limitations

The High-Low Method, while useful, has some limitations:

  • Relies on only two data points, which may not accurately represent cost behavior over a wider range of activities.
  • Assumes that all variable costs change at the same rate, which may not reflect the complexity of real-world scenarios.
  • May not provide a comprehensive understanding of cost behavior for businesses with intricate cost structures.

Conclusion

The High-Low Method is a simple and easy-to-use tool for cost behavior analysis, but it has some limitations. It is based on only two data points and assumes that all variable costs change at the same rate. Despite these limitations, it can be a useful tool for identifying the fixed and variable components of a cost.
