Horizontal Analysis vs Vertical Analysis

What is Horizontal Analysis?

Horizontal analysis, also known as trend analysis, is a financial analysis technique used to compare financial data over different periods of time. The primary goal of this analysis is to identify trends and patterns that can provide insights into the company's financial health and performance. By examining financial data over multiple reporting periods, horizontal analysis can help management and investors understand how a company's financial position has changed over time.

Horizontal analysis typically involves comparing financial figures such as revenues, expenses, and net income across several reporting periods. For instance, a company might use horizontal analysis to compare its revenues for the past five years to determine whether its revenue is increasing, decreasing, or remaining relatively stable over time. Similarly, it can analyze the trend in expenses to identify any significant changes or fluctuations that might impact the company's profitability.

This analysis can also be presented in a table or graph format, making it easier to visualize and interpret the data. By analyzing the trends in financial data, management can make informed decisions about the company's operations and financial strategy. For instance, if a company notices a decline in revenue over time, it might decide to invest in marketing or product development to boost sales. Similarly, if expenses are increasing faster than revenue, the company might need to cut costs or find ways to increase revenue to maintain profitability.

In summary, horizontal analysis is a valuable tool for financial analysis that can provide insights into a company's financial health and performance over time. By examining trends in financial data, management and investors can make informed decisions about the company's financial strategy and operations.

What is Vertical Analysis?

Vertical analysis, also known as common-size analysis, is a financial analysis technique used to compare different components of financial statements to a key metric, typically total revenue or total assets. This method involves expressing each line item as a percentage of a base figure, allowing for easy comparison across different companies or industries. Vertical analysis helps in understanding the composition and proportion of various elements within a financial statement.

For instance, vertical analysis of an income statement would express each expense category (e.g., cost of goods sold, selling and administrative expenses) as a percentage of total revenue. By expressing each expense as a percentage of total revenue, management and investors can assess the relative importance of each expense category in relation to the company's total revenue. This analysis can help identify any significant changes or trends in expenses, allowing management to make informed decisions about the company's operations and financial strategy.

Similarly, a vertical analysis of a balance sheet would express each asset and liability category as a percentage of total assets or total liabilities and equity. This analysis helps in understanding the composition of a company's assets and liabilities, providing insights into the company's financial structure and liquidity.

Vertical analysis can be useful in several ways, including:

  • Comparing financial statements across different companies or industries, even if the companies have different scales of operations.
  • Identifying trends and changes in financial statement items over time.
  • Analyzing the composition and proportion of various elements within a financial statement.
  • Providing insights into a company's financial structure and liquidity.

In summary, vertical analysis is a valuable tool for financial analysis that can provide insights into the composition and proportion of various elements within a financial statement. By expressing each line item as a percentage of a base figure, vertical analysis allows for easy comparison across different companies or industries, helping management and investors make informed decisions about the company's operations and financial strategy.

Horizontal analysis vs. Vertical analysis

Altogether, there are a few main similarities between horizontal analysis and vertical analysis, including:

  • Both horizontal and vertical analysis can be used by internal and external stakeholders.
  • Both forms of analysis can help you pick out trends and patterns in financial data and develop strategies.
  • Both forms of analysis can help you analyze various financial statements, including balance sheets and income statements.

However, there are also some differences between the two that are important to understand. The primary differences between horizontal and vertical analysis include:

  • Horizontal analysis is performed horizontally across time periods, while vertical analysis is performed vertically inside of a column.
  • Horizontal analysis represents changes over years or periods, while vertical analysis represents amounts as percentages of a base figure.
  • Horizontal analysis usually examines many reporting periods, while vertical analysis typically focuses on one reporting period.
  • Horizontal analysis can help you compare a company's current financial status to its past status, while vertical analysis can help you compare one company's financial status to another's.
