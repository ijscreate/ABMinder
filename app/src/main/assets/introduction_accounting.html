Introduction to Accounting

Accounting is the process of recording, summarizing, analyzing, and reporting financial transactions of a business or organization. It provides insights into the financial health and performance of a company, enabling stakeholders to make informed decisions.

Key aspects of accounting include:

  • Recording: Accounting involves systematically recording financial transactions, such as sales, purchases, and expenses, in an organized manner.
  • Summarizing: Once transactions are recorded, they are summarized into financial statements, such as the balance sheet, income statement, and cash flow statement, which provide an overview of the company's financial position and performance.
  • Analyzing: Accountants analyze financial data to identify trends, patterns, and areas of concern. They may use various financial ratios and metrics to assess the company's performance and compare it to industry benchmarks.
  • Reporting: Finally, accounting involves preparing and presenting financial reports to stakeholders, including investors, creditors, regulators, and management. These reports provide transparency and accountability, allowing stakeholders to evaluate the company's financial health and make informed decisions.

Why Accounting is Important:

  1. Financial Statements: Accounting provides financial statements that summarize a company's financial position, performance, and cash flows. These statements are used by investors, creditors, and regulatory agencies to make informed decisions about the financial health and performance of a company.
  2. Compliance: Accounting ensures compliance with financial regulations and laws. Financial accountants follow generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP) and international financial reporting standards (IFRS) to prepare financial statements. Tax accountants follow the Internal Revenue Code (IRC) and regulations issued by the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) and other taxing authorities to ensure compliance with tax laws and regulations.
  3. Decision Making: Accounting provides financial information that is used to make informed decisions. Financial statements, budgets, forecasts, and financial analysis tools are used by management to make strategic decisions about pricing, production, and profitability.
  4. Transparency: Accounting provides transparency to stakeholders. Financial statements and reports provide a clear picture of a company's financial position, performance, and cash flows. This transparency helps build trust and credibility with stakeholders, such as investors, creditors, and regulatory agencies.
  5. Risk Management: Accounting helps manage risk by providing financial information that is used to identify and mitigate risks. Financial analysis tools, such as variance analysis and sensitivity analysis, are used to identify risks and opportunities.
  6. Strategic Planning: Accounting is used in strategic planning by providing financial information that is used to make informed decisions about the future direction of a company. Budgets, forecasts, and financial projections are used to develop strategic plans and goals.
  7. Communication: Accounting is used to communicate financial information to stakeholders. Financial statements, reports, and presentations are used to communicate financial information to investors, creditors, regulatory agencies, and other stakeholders.

What Are the Different Types of Accounting?

Financial Accounting:

Financial accounting refers to the processes used to generate interim and annual financial statements. The results of all financial transactions that occur during an accounting period are summarized in the balance sheet, income statement, and cash flow statement. The financial statements of most companies are audited annually by an external CPA firm. For some, such as publicly-traded companies, audits are a legal requirement. However, lenders also typically require the results of an external audit annually as part of their debt covenants. Therefore, most companies will have annual audits for one reason or another. Financial accounting is governed by generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP) and international financial reporting standards (IFRS) in different countries. The goal of financial accounting is to provide financial information to external stakeholders, such as investors, creditors, and regulatory agencies, to make informed decisions about the financial health and performance of a company.

Managerial Accounting:

Managerial accounting uses much of the same data as financial accounting, but it organizes and utilizes information in different ways. Namely, in managerial accounting, an accountant generates monthly or quarterly reports that a business's management team can use to make decisions about how the business operates. Managerial accounting also encompasses many other facets of accounting, including budgeting, forecasting, and various financial analysis tools. Essentially, any information that may be useful to management falls underneath this umbrella. Managerial accounting is not governed by GAAP or IFRS, but rather by the needs of the management team. The goal of managerial accounting is to provide financial information to internal stakeholders, such as managers and business owners, to make informed decisions about the operations and performance of a company.

Cost Accounting:

Cost accounting is a subset of managerial accounting that focuses on determining the costs associated with producing a product or providing a service. Cost accountants use various methods, such as activity-based costing and job order costing, to allocate costs to products or services. The goal of cost accounting is to provide financial information to management to make informed decisions about pricing, production, and profitability. Cost accountants also work with management to develop budgets and forecasts, and to identify opportunities to reduce costs and improve efficiency.

Tax Accounting:

While financial accountants often use one set of rules to report the financial position of a company, tax accountants often use a different set of rules. These rules are set at the federal, state, or local level based on what return is being filed. Tax accounts balance compliance with reporting rules while also attempting to minimize a company's tax liability through thoughtful strategic decision-making. A tax accountant often oversees the entire tax process of a company: the strategic creation of the organization chart, the operations, the compliance, the reporting, and the remittance of tax liability. Tax accounting is governed by the Internal Revenue Code (IRC) and regulations issued by the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) and other taxing authorities. The goal of tax accounting is to ensure compliance with tax laws and regulations while minimizing a company's tax liability.

History of Accounting

It is believed that the history of accounting dates back thousands of years and can be traced to ancient civilizations. One of the earliest developments of accounting is thought to have occurred in ancient Mesopotamia, where people followed systems of writing and counting money, likely related to taxation and trading activities of temples.

More evidence of the development of accounting can be found during the reign of Emperor Augustus (63BCâ€”14AD) in ancient Rome. Detailed financial information regarding the stewardship of Roman resources was recorded, including public revenues, treasury amounts, and tax officials' records, in documents such as the Res Gestae Divi Augusti.

A significant milestone in accounting history occurred in fourteenth-century Italy with the dissemination of double-entry bookkeeping by Luca Pacioli. Pacioli is often referred to as the father of modern accounting due to his work. The double-entry bookkeeping system, which records both debits and credits for each transaction, was introduced in Pacioli's Summa de Arithmetica, Geometria, Proportioni et Proportionalita.

The modern profession of the chartered accountant originated in Scotland in the nineteenth century with the granting of a royal charter to the Institute of Accountants in Glasgow by Queen Victoria. Today, accounting standards such as the Philippine Financial Reporting Standard (PFRS) and the Philippine Accounting Standards (PAS) guide accountants in their practice of the profession.
