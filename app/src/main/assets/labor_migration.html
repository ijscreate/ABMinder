Labor Migration and Overseas Filipino Workers (OFWs)

Labor migration and Overseas Filipino Workers (OFWs) play a crucial role in the Philippines' economy and social fabric. Migration is not only a livelihood strategy for millions of Filipinos seeking better employment opportunities but also a significant contributor to poverty reduction and economic development.

History and Growth of Labor Migration

The Philippines has a long history of labor migration, with OFWs representing a subset of Overseas Filipinos who are temporary migrants. Over the last thirty years, overseas labor migration has become increasingly important, reducing unemployment and strengthening US dollar reserves. The government of Ferdinand Marcos recognized the opportunity presented by the demand for workers in Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) countries and other regions, leading to the establishment of the Labor Code of the Philippines in 1974 to facilitate organized international labor migration. Initially intended as a temporary measure to address economic challenges, labor migration has continued to grow significantly over the years. The flow of OFWs surged past 1 million annually beginning in 2006, with more than 1,844,000 Filipinos working abroad in 2015 alone.

Global Distribution and Contributions of OFWs

Filipinos are present across the globe due to work opportunities, with the Middle East receiving the largest share of OFWs followed by Asia. The Philippines has integrated itself into the global economy through government-sponsored labor export programs that send more than 500,000 Filipinos abroad each year to work in various countries and territories. These workers engage in a wide range of occupations, from doctors and nurses to janitors and maids, contributing significantly to their host countries' economies.

Government Policies and Economic Impact

The Philippine government's efforts at economic growth and development are closely tied to labor migration as a means of addressing unemployment and generating foreign exchange reserves. While labor-exporting countries like the Philippines retain control over their migration policies, international labor migration is influenced by specific demands within immigrant-receiving societies. The changing structural conditions in destination countries significantly impact the procurement of migrant labor, shaping patterns of employment and occupational structures within global labor markets.
