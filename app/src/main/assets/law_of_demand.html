Law of Demand: Definition, Principles, and Examples

What Is the Law of Demand?

The Law of Demand is a fundamental principle in economics that states that, all else being equal, as the price of a good or service decreases, the quantity demanded increases, and vice versa.

The law of demand states that the quantity purchased varies inversely with price. In other words, the higher the price, the lower the quantity demanded. This occurs because of diminishing marginal utility. That is, consumers use the first units of an economic good they purchase to serve their most urgent needs first, then they use each additional unit of the good to serve successively lower-valued ends.

Key Principles of the Law of Demand

The Law of Demand is based on the following key principles:

  • Negative Relationship: There is a negative or inverse relationship between the price of a good or service and the quantity demanded.
  • Ceteris Paribus: The law assumes that all other factors influencing demand remain constant.
  • Continuum: The law applies over a range of prices, creating a demand curve.

Understanding the Law of Demand

Economics involves the study of how people use limited means to satisfy unlimited wants. The law of demand focuses on those unlimited wants. Naturally, people prioritize more urgent wants and needs over less urgent ones in their economic behavior, and this carries over into how people choose among the limited means available to them.

For any economic good, the first unit of that good that a consumer gets their hands on will tend to be used to satisfy the most urgent need the consumer has that that good can satisfy.

For example, consider a castaway on a desert island who obtains a six-pack of bottled fresh water that washes up onshore. The first bottle will be used to satisfy the castawayâ€™s most urgently felt need, which is most likely drinking water to avoid dying of thirst.

Factors Influencing Demand

Several factors can influence demand and shift the demand curve:

  • Price of Substitutes and Complements: The prices of substitute goods and complementary goods affect the demand for a particular good.
  • Consumer Income: Changes in consumer income can influence the demand for normal and inferior goods.
  • Tastes and Preferences: Changes in consumer preferences can impact the demand for certain goods.
  • Population Changes: An increase or decrease in the population can affect overall demand.
  • Future Expectations: Consumer expectations about future prices and economic conditions can influence current demand.

Examples of the Law of Demand

Let's explore a few examples to illustrate the Law of Demand:

  1. As the price of smartphones decreases, the quantity demanded by consumers increases.
  2. When the price of gasoline rises, consumers tend to decrease their quantity demanded by carpooling or using public transportation.
  3. If the price of a popular brand of coffee increases, consumers may shift to a more affordable brand, leading to a decrease in demand for the expensive coffee.
