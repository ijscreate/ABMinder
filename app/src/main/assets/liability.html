Liabilities

Liabilities, in financial accounting, refer to financial obligations that a person or company owes to others, typically involving the transfer of economic benefits over time. They can range from simple debts like car payments to large loans for business operations. Liabilities are crucial in business finance, often used to fund operations and expansions. They are categorized into current liabilities, due within a year, and non-current liabilities, not expected to be settled within a year. Proper management of liabilities is essential for a company's financial health, impacting liquidity, capital structure, and overall performance

A liability is incurred to realize a transfer of economic benefits, with the recipient of economic benefits incurring the liability. For example, when a business buys goods on credit from its supplier, the supplier completes a sale to its customer and collects cash.

Liabilities can be settled through various means, including cash payment, distribution of other assets, rendering of services, substitution with another liability, or conversion of the liability into ownership interest of the lender in the borrower’s entity.

Liabilities are financial obligations or debts that a person or company owes to another party. In accounting, liabilities are categorized into two main types: current liabilities and non-current liabilities.

Current Liabilities

  • Accounts Payable: Money owed to suppliers or vendors for the products or services purchased on credit.
  • Short-Term Loans: Funds borrowed that must be repaid within a year.
  • Accrued Expenses: These include the expenses that have been incurred but have not yet been paid, such as salary, utilities, or taxes.
  • Unearned Revenue: Revenue earned in advance for goods or services that have not yet been delivered to the consumer.
  • Current Portion of Long-Term Debt: The sum of long-term debt due within the following calendar year.

Non-Current Liabilities

  • Long-Term Loans: Debt obligations with maturities of more than a year.
  • Bonds Payable: Debt instruments issued by a firm to raise funds, typically with a fixed interest rate and maturity date.
  • Lease Liabilities: Long-term lease arrangements that involve regular payments over a long period of time.
  • Pension Liabilities: Liabilities incurred as a result of employee retirement benefits.
  • Deferred Tax Liabilities: Taxes that have been incurred but not yet paid.

Here are examples of liabilities:

1. Accounts Receivable: A business has an outstanding balance of ₱200,000 from its customers for products sold on credit.

2. Bank Loan: The business has a loan from a bank amounting to ₱500,000, with interest payable at a rate of 12% per annum.

3. Income Tax Payable: The business owes ₱100,000 in income tax to the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) for the current financial year.

These liabilities are recorded on the right side of the balance sheet, along with other liabilities and equity.

Proper management of liabilities is crucial for a business's financial health and stability, as it ensures that the business can meet its financial obligations in a timely manner.
