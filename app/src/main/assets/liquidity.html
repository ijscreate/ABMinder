Liquidity

What is Liquidity?

Liquidity refers to the ease with which an asset, or security, can be converted into ready cash without affecting its market price. Liquidity is characterized by a high level of trading activity. Assets that can be easily bought or sold are known as liquid assets. Conversely, assets that take longer to sell are considered illiquid or non-liquid.

Liquidity is essential for the smooth functioning of financial markets and is a key consideration for investors when making investment decisions.

Understanding Liquidity

Market Liquidity

Market liquidity pertains to how easily assets can be bought and sold in a market at stable and transparent prices. In contrast to the illiquid market for rare books and refrigerators, stock markets typically exhibit higher market liquidity. A high trade volume not dominated by selling results in bid and ask prices being closely aligned. Tight bid-ask spreads indicate high liquidity, while widening spreads signify lower liquidity. Real estate markets generally have lower liquidity compared to stock markets, while the liquidity of other assets like derivatives, currencies, or commodities depends on factors such as market size and availability of trading platforms.

Accounting Liquidity

Accounting liquidity gauges an individual's or company's ability to meet financial obligations using their liquid assets. It involves comparing liquid assets to current liabilities or debts due within a year.

Measuring Liquidity

Financial analysts assess a firm's capacity to fulfill short-term obligations using liquid assets. Ratios greater than one are usually preferred in these analyses.

Current Ratio

The current ratio is a basic measure comparing current assets against current liabilities, indicating the firm's ability to cover short-term obligations.

Current Ratio = Current Assets Current Liabilities \text{Current Ratio} = \frac{\text{Current Assets}}{\text{Current Liabilities}}

Quick Ratio (Acid-Test Ratio)

The quick ratio is more stringent than the current ratio as it excludes certain current assets like inventories. It focuses on highly liquid assets such as cash equivalents and accounts receivable.

Quick Ratio = Current Assets - Inventories Current Liabilities \text{Quick Ratio} = \frac{\text{Current Assets - Inventories}}{\text{Current Liabilities}}

Acid-Test Ratio (Variation)

A variation of the quick ratio further refines the calculation by subtracting inventory and prepaid costs from current assets to determine liquidity.

Acid-Test Ratio (Variation) = Current Assets - Inventories - Prepaid Costs Current Liabilities \text{Acid-Test Ratio (Variation)} = \frac{\text{Current Assets - Inventories - Prepaid Costs}}{\text{Current Liabilities}}

Cash Ratio

The cash ratio is the most stringent liquidity ratio, considering only cash and cash equivalents as liquid assets. It evaluates an entity's ability to remain solvent in emergencies by focusing solely on its cash reserves.

Cash Ratio = Cash + Cash Equivalents Current Liabilities \text{Cash Ratio} = \frac{\text{Cash + Cash Equivalents}}{\text{Current Liabilities}}

Liquidity Example in the Philippines

In the Philippines, liquidity can be observed in various financial markets and transactions. Let's consider an example illustrating liquidity in the Philippine stock market:

Trading Stocks on the Philippine Stock Exchange (PSE)

The Philippine Stock Exchange (PSE) serves as the primary platform for buying and selling stocks of publicly listed companies in the Philippines. Liquidity in the stock market refers to the ease and efficiency with which stocks can be bought or sold without significantly impacting their market price.

Suppose an investor wants to purchase shares of a company listed on the PSE. They can place an order through their brokerage account, specifying the quantity of shares they wish to buy and the price they are willing to pay. Similarly, if an investor wants to sell their shares, they can place a sell order with their broker.

Liquidity in the PSE is evident in the following ways:

  1. High Trade Volume: The PSE experiences significant trading activity, with numerous buy and sell orders executed throughout the trading day. This high volume of transactions indicates liquidity, as there are ample opportunities for investors to buy or sell stocks at prevailing market prices.
  2. Tight Bid-Ask Spreads: Liquidity is also reflected in the bid-ask spread, which is the difference between the highest price a buyer is willing to pay (bid price) and the lowest price a seller is willing to accept (ask price). In a liquid market, bid-ask spreads tend to be narrow, indicating that buyers and sellers are closely aligned in their pricing expectations.
  3. Availability of Market Participants: The PSE attracts a diverse range of market participants, including individual investors, institutional investors, traders, and market makers. This broad participation contributes to liquidity by increasing the likelihood of finding counterparties for trade orders.
  4. Market Depth: Liquidity is further enhanced by the depth of the market, which refers to the volume of buy and sell orders at various price levels. A deep market indicates robust liquidity, as there are sufficient orders to accommodate large trades without significantly impacting prices.

Overall, the active trading environment, narrow bid-ask spreads, diverse participant base, and market depth demonstrate liquidity in the Philippine stock market. This liquidity facilitates efficient price discovery, supports investor confidence, and enables seamless execution of buy and sell orders, contributing to the smooth functioning of the country's capital markets.

Importance of Liquidity

Liquidity is essential for several reasons:

  1. Trading and Market Efficiency: High liquidity facilitates efficient trading and price discovery in financial markets. It allows investors to enter and exit positions quickly, reducing market volatility and enhancing market efficiency.
  2. Risk Management: Liquidity plays a vital role in managing investment risk. It enables investors to adjust their portfolios promptly in response to changing market conditions or unexpected events.
  3. Access to Cash: Adequate liquidity ensures that individuals and businesses have access to cash when needed, preventing financial distress during times of financial stress.

Understanding liquidity is crucial for investors, businesses, and policymakers. It helps in making informed decisions about investments, managing risk, and ensuring the smooth functioning of financial markets.
