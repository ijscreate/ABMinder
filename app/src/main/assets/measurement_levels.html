Levels of Measurement

Understanding the different levels of measurement in data analysis

Nominal Level:

At this level, data can only be categorized into distinct groups without any inherent order.

Examples of nominal scales include:

  • City of birth
  • Gender
  • Ethnicity
  • Car brands
  • Marital status

Ordinal Level:

Data can be categorized and ranked, but we cannot infer the exact intervals between rankings.

For instance:

  • Ranking the top 5 Olympic medalists (though we don’t know the exact gap between their wins)
  • Language ability (e.g., beginner, intermediate, fluent)
  • Likert-type questions (e.g., very dissatisfied to very satisfied)

Interval Level:

At this level, we can categorize, rank, and infer equal intervals between neighboring data points.

However, there is no true zero point. Zero degrees does not signify an absolute absence of temperature; it’s arbitrary.

Examples include:

  • Test scores (e.g., IQ or exams)
  • Personality inventories
  • Temperature in Fahrenheit or Celsius

Ratio Level:

The most precise level, where we can categorize, rank, infer equal intervals, and have a true zero point.

A true zero means an absolute lack of the variable being measured.

Examples of ratio scales:

  • Height (in centimeters or inches)
  • Weight (in kilograms or pounds)
  • Income (in dollars)

Understanding Levels of Measurement in a Survey Analysis

You are a market researcher working for a consumer goods company. Your team is tasked with conducting a survey to gather data on consumer preferences for a new product line.

To design an effective survey, you need to understand the different levels of measurement and choose appropriate question types based on the nature of the data.

Example scenario:

  1. Nominal Level:
    You include a question in the survey asking respondents to select their favorite color from a list of options. The colors (e.g., red, blue, green) represent distinct categories without any inherent order. This data is at the nominal level.
  2. Ordinal Level:
    Another question in the survey asks respondents to rate their satisfaction with the company's customer service on a scale from 1 to 5, with 1 being "very dissatisfied" and 5 being "very satisfied." While the responses are ranked, the exact intervals between the ratings are not uniform. This data is at the ordinal level.
  3. Interval Level:
    You include a question asking respondents to rate their likelihood of recommending the company's products to others on a scale from 0 to 10, where 0 represents "not likely at all" and 10 represents "extremely likely." The intervals between the ratings are equal, but there is no true zero point on the scale. This data is at the interval level.
  4. Ratio Level:
    Finally, you ask respondents to provide their annual household income in dollars. This data is at the ratio level because it has a true zero point (absolute absence of income), and equal intervals between values allow for meaningful comparisons and mathematical operations.
