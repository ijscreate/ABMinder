Models and Frameworks of Social Responsibility

Organizations should operate ethically and with concern for social, cultural, economic, and environmental issues according to the concept of social responsibility. This can manifest in various ways, from corporate donations to community outreach programs.

However, the interpretation of social responsibility depends on the philosophical outlook of entrepreneurs. Two prominent models are:

The Stockholder Theory

The Stockholder Theory, also known as the Shareholder Theory, is a business philosophy that prioritizes the interests of shareholders above all other stakeholders. This theory posits that the primary objective of a corporation is to maximize profits for its shareholders, and any action that does not contribute to this goal may be viewed negatively.

The theory is based on the idea that shareholders are the owners of the company, and they have the right to expect a return on their investment. This return is typically in the form of dividends or capital gains, and it is the responsibility of the company's management to ensure that these returns are maximized.

One of the key features of the Stockholder Theory is the concept of stockholder voting rights. These rights give shareholders the power to vote on corporate matters, such as the election of directors, the issuance of additional shares, and corporate takeovers. By exercising these rights, shareholders can protect their investment and ensure that the company is being managed in a way that maximizes their returns.

For example, in the case of a corporate takeover, the shareholders of the target company typically have the final say on whether the transaction goes through. This allows them to protect their investment from potentially detrimental changes of control. Similarly, in the election of directors, stockholder voting rights can dictate the composition of the board and ensure that it is aligned with the interests of the shareholders.

However, the Stockholder Theory has been criticized for its narrow focus on shareholder interests. Critics argue that it ignores the needs and interests of other stakeholders, such as employees, customers, and the community. They argue that a corporation has a broader social responsibility to consider the impact of its actions on all of its stakeholders, not just its shareholders.

Stakeholder Theory

According to Edward Freeman's stakeholder theory, a company's stakeholders comprise anyone who is affected by the company's operations. Customers, employees, suppliers, political action organizations, environmental groups, local communities, the media, financial institutions, governmental groups, and other stakeholders, according to Dr. Freeman, are "those groups without whose support the corporation would cease to exist." This perspective depicts the corporate environment as an ecosystem of interconnected groups that must all be considered and satisfied in order for the organization to remain healthy and successful in the long run. In his books, Dr. Freeman explains how a thriving firm never loses sight of everyone who contributes to its success. According to the stakeholder theory, if a company treats its employees badly, it will eventually fail. If it imposes its initiatives on communities, the same thing will almost certainly happen. "To genuinely prosper, a company cannot ignore any of its stakeholders." In an interview, Dr. Freeman stated that "There may be short-term profits, but the company will not exist if stakeholders are unsatisfied and feel betrayed."

Considerations for Stakeholders:

  1. Employees: Employees want to be treated as equals, be justly compensated, and work normal hours. Employee attitudes and buy-in to the company will deteriorate if the company underpays them or assigns them long and difficult work shifts. Negative consequences include a high rate of attrition, negative word-of-mouth among potential employees, and a weakened organization.
  2. Suppliers: Suppliers should be treated and paid appropriately, as neglecting them can lead to adverse outcomes similar to those observed with employees. The condo firm should conduct due diligence on how the supply companies conduct business for long-term success.
  3. Manufacturers: Working conditions and wages of manufacturers and their employees must be fair and equitable for long-term success.
  4. Environmentalists: Environmental concerns should be addressed transparently to ensure the protection of the local ecology, keeping stakeholders informed and involved in project plans and developments.
  5. Housing activists: Local activists' concerns, such as parking availability, services for residents, and displacement of long-time residents, should be considered to gain their support and enhance community relations.
  6. Governmental bodies: Compliance with governmental regulations and concerns, including density, environmental, and design restrictions, is crucial for project approval and ongoing support.
  7. Neighbors: Consideration for the quality of life of neighboring residents, including parking, green space, and shared amenities, is essential for maintaining or enhancing community relations.
