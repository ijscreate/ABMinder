Understanding the Process

The process of adding fractions involves consolidating parts of a whole, a skill essential for tasks such as inventory management and financial analysis. Consider a scenario where a business needs to evaluate its daily inventory turnover. If 1/4 of the stock is sold on Monday and an additional 3/8 on Tuesday, the objective is to determine the cumulative proportion sold over the two days.

Example 1:

Step 1: Finding a Common Denominator

  • Identify the least common multiple of 4 and 8, which is 8.

Step 2: Rewriting Fractions with the Common Denominator

  • 1/4 becomes 2/8.
  • 3/8 remains 3/8.

Step 3: Adding the Fractions

  • 2/8 + 3/8 = 5/8.

Therefore, the company has successfully sold 5/8 of its inventory over the two days.

Example 2: Combining Fractional Investments

Step 1: Finding a Common Denominator

  • Identify the least common multiple of 3 and 5, which is 15.

Step 2: Rewriting Fractions with the Common Denominator

  • 1/3 becomes 5/15.
  • 2/5 becomes 6/15.

Step 3: Adding the Fractions

  • 5/15 + 6/15 = 11/15.

Therefore, the combined ownership stake in the project is 11/15.

Subtracting Fractions

In this part of the lesson, we will explore the nuanced process of subtracting fractions, a critical skill for maintaining financial accuracy in business mathematics.

Understanding the Process

Subtracting fractions is crucial for tasks such as calculating profit margins, determining changes in quantities, and analyzing financial data. Imagine a scenario where a business, mindful of budget constraints, has 3/10 of its budget remaining at the end of the first quarter. If it spends an additional 2/5 of the remaining budget in the second quarter, the goal is to ascertain the residual budget after the second quarter.

Example 1:

Step 1: Finding a Common Denominator

  • Identify the least common multiple of 5 and 10, which is 10.

Step 2: Rewriting Fractions with the Common Denominator

  • 3/10 remains 3/10.
  • 2/5 becomes 4/10.

Step 3: Subtracting the Fractions

  • 3/10 - 4/10 = -1/10.

The business, at the conclusion of the second quarter, faced a deficit equivalent to -1/10 of the budget.
