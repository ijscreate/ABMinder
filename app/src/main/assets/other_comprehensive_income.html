Another Comprehensive Income

Another Comprehensive Income is composed of income and expenses that are not presented as profit or loss as required by the Philippine Financial Reporting Standards. These are special accounts such as:

  • Unrealized gain or loss on investment in equity instrument measured at fair value through other comprehensive income
  • Gain or loss from translating the financial statements of a foreign operation
  • Change in revaluation surplus
  • Unrealized gain or loss from derivative contracts designated as cash flow hedge
  • Actuarial gain or loss on defined benefits plan by the full recognition approach

Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) is a financial term that refers to revenues, expenses, gains, and losses that have not yet been realized. It is an important measure of a company's value, particularly for larger corporations. OCI includes items such as unrealized gains or losses on financial assets, gains or losses from translating the financial statements of a foreign operation, revaluation surplus, unrealized gains or losses from derivative contracts designated as cash flow hedge, and actuarial gain or loss on defined benefit plans.

In the Philippines, OCI is not part of retained earnings but is instead reported separately on the balance sheet as accumulated other comprehensive income (AOCI). It is not the same as comprehensive income, which is the sum of net income and OCI. OCI is not included in the income statement but is instead reported in the balance sheet.

A common example of OCI is a bond portfolio that is not classified as held-to-maturity. If the value of the bonds in the portfolio changes, the unrealized gain or loss is recognized in OCI until the bonds are sold. At that point, the gain or loss is realized and flows through to the income statement.

OCI is an important financial measure because it provides insight into a company's financial performance beyond its core operations. It is particularly useful for investors who are interested in understanding the potential for realized gains or losses on the income statement down the road. By tracking OCI over time, investors can gain a more complete picture of a company's financial health and potential for future growth.
