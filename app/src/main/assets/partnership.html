Partnership

Definition of Partnership

A partnership is a business structure in which two or more individuals manage and operate a business in accordance with the terms and objectives set out in a Partnership Deed. Each partner shares a portion of the partnership's profits and losses and each partner is personally liable for the debt and obligations of the partnership.

Key Takeaways

  • A partnership is a formal arrangement by two or more parties to manage and operate a business and share its profits.
  • There are several types of partnership arrangements, including general partnerships, limited partnerships, and limited liability partnerships.
  • In a general partnership, each partner is personally liable for the debts and obligations of the business.
  • In a limited partnership, there are both general partners and limited partners. General partners have full liability for the partnership's debts, while limited partners' liabilities are limited to their investment.
  • In a limited liability partnership, all partners have limited liability, protecting them from potential lawsuits against the partnership.

Understanding Partnership

A partnership is a business with multiple owners, each of whom has invested in the business. Some partnerships include individuals who work in the business, while other may include partners who have limited participation and also limited liability.

Partnerships are generally easy to establish and can be formed with minimal formalities. Partnerships are not typically subject to the same extensive regulatory requirements and administrative filings as corporations. However, partnerships must file an annual information return to report income, deductions, gains, losses, etc.

Types of Partnerships

In a general partnership, all partners are jointly and severally liable for the debts and liabilities of the partnership. This means that each partner is individually responsible for the partnership's debts and liabilities, and if one partner is unable to pay their share, the other partners may be required to cover the deficit.

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Easy to establish: General partnerships are relatively easy and inexpensive to set up compared to other business structures.
  • Shared decision-making: Partners can share the responsibilities of managing the business, leveraging each other's skills and expertise.
  • Shared financial burden: Partners can pool their resources to invest in the business and share the financial risks.
  • Pass-through taxation: Profits and losses are passed through to the partners, who report them on their individual tax returns, simplifying tax compliance.
  • Flexibility: General partnerships offer flexibility in management and decision-making, allowing partners to adapt quickly to changing market conditions.
  • Unlimited liability: Partners have unlimited personal liability for the debts and obligations of the partnership, putting their personal assets at risk.
  • Shared profits: Partners must share the profits of the business equally or as agreed upon in the partnership agreement, regardless of their individual contributions.
  • Conflict between partners: Disagreements between partners can arise over management decisions, profit distribution, or other aspects of the business.
  • Joint decision-making: Partners must make decisions collectively, which can lead to delays or disagreements if partners cannot reach a consensus.
  • Partnership dissolutions: General partnerships may dissolve if one partner decides to leave the business or if the partnership agreement expires, leading to potential disruptions in operations.

In a limited liability partnership, the liability of the general partners is limited to the amount they have invested in the partnership. This means that if the partnership incurs debts or liabilities, the general partners are only responsible for the amount they have invested, and not for the entire debt or liability of the partnership.

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Limited liability: Partners have limited personal liability for the debts and obligations of the LLP, protecting their personal assets from business liabilities.
  • Separate legal entity: An LLP is a separate legal entity from its partners, allowing it to enter into contracts, own property, and conduct business in its own name.
  • Pass-through taxation: Like general partnerships, LLPs are taxed as pass-through entities, meaning that profits and losses are passed through to the partners and reported on their individual tax returns.
  • Flexibility in management: LLPs offer flexibility in management, allowing partners to participate in the day-to-day operations of the business or take a more hands-off approach, depending on their preferences.
  • Attractiveness to professionals: LLPs are often favored by professionals such as lawyers, accountants, and consultants, who wish to form a partnership while limiting their personal liability.
  • Complex formation: Forming an LLP may be more complex and costly than forming a general partnership, as it requires registration with the appropriate government authorities and compliance with specific legal requirements.
  • Regulatory requirements: LLPs are subject to regulatory requirements and reporting obligations, including filing annual returns and maintaining proper accounting records, which may involve additional administrative burdens and costs.
  • Shared decision-making: Like general partnerships, LLPs require partners to make decisions collectively, which can lead to conflicts or delays in decision-making if partners cannot reach a consensus.
  • Perception of credibility: Some stakeholders, such as lenders or customers, may perceive LLPs as less credible or stable compared to corporations, which may affect the LLP's ability to attract financing or business opportunities.
  • Continuity concerns: LLPs may face continuity concerns if partners decide to leave the partnership or if there are changes in ownership, which could disrupt business operations or lead to the dissolution of the LLP.

In a limited partnership, there is usually one general partner who is responsible for the management and day-to-day operation of the partnership, and one or more silent partners who do not participate in the management or operation of the partnership. The silent partners are not responsible for the debts or liabilities of the partnership, and they do not have any say in the management or operation of the partnership.

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Limited liability for limited partners: Limited partners have limited personal liability for the debts and obligations of the partnership, protecting their personal assets from business liabilities beyond their initial investment.
  • Passive investment: Limited partners can invest capital in the partnership without being actively involved in its management or day-to-day operations, allowing them to benefit from the partnership's profits without assuming significant management responsibilities.
  • Access to capital: Limited partnerships can attract investors by offering limited liability to limited partners, making them more attractive investment opportunities compared to general partnerships or sole proprietorships.
  • Expertise and resources: Limited partnerships allow general partners to access additional capital, expertise, and resources from limited partners, which can help support the growth and expansion of the business.
  • Tax advantages: Limited partnerships are taxed as pass-through entities, meaning that profits and losses are passed through to the partners and reported on their individual tax returns, potentially resulting in tax benefits for partners.
  • Unlimited liability for general partners: General partners have unlimited personal liability for the debts and obligations of the partnership, meaning they are personally responsible for any losses incurred by the partnership beyond their initial investment.
  • Management responsibilities: General partners are responsible for managing the day-to-day operations of the partnership and making key business decisions, which can be time-consuming and demanding.
  • Complexity and legal requirements: Limited partnerships may be subject to complex legal requirements and regulatory obligations, including filing formation documents, maintaining proper records, and complying with securities laws, which can involve significant administrative burdens and costs.
  • Conflict potential: Limited partnerships may be susceptible to conflicts between general and limited partners, particularly regarding decision-making authority, profit-sharing arrangements, and management responsibilities, which can strain the partnership's relationships and hinder its operations.
  • Market perception: Limited partnerships may face challenges in attracting investors or obtaining financing due to concerns about the unlimited liability of general partners and the potential for conflicts between partners, which may affect the partnership's credibility and reputation in the market.

When drafting a partnership agreement, it is important to clearly define the roles and responsibilities of each partner, as well as the specific terms and conditions under which the partnership will operate. This can include details about profit sharing, expulsion clauses, and the rights and responsibilities of each partner in the management and operation of the partnership.

By understanding the different types of partnerships and their associated liabilities, you can make informed decisions about the best structure for your business and its partnership arrangements.

Example

Let's take the example of a law firm, Smith & Jones, which is a general partnership between two attorneys, John Smith and Jane Jones. John and Jane have been practicing law individually for several years and have decided to combine their practices to better serve their clients and expand their business. They each contribute P50,000 in capital to start the business and share profits and losses equally.

As general partners, John and Jane are both responsible for managing the business and have unlimited personal liability for the debts and obligations of the partnership. This means that if the partnership incurs debt or is sued, both John and Jane are personally liable for the amount owed, regardless of their ownership percentage.

To monitor the financial health of their business, John and Jane use accounting information to track income, expenses, and partnership capital. They use financial statements, such as income statements and balance sheets, to assess the profitability and financial position of the business. This information is also important for tax reporting purposes, as partnerships are considered pass-through entities for tax purposes, meaning the partnership itself is not taxed, but the partners are taxed on their share of the partnership's income.

John and Jane also use accounting information to make informed decisions about the use of resources, such as whether to purchase new office equipment or hire additional staff. They may use budgeting and forecasting tools to project future cash flows and make informed decisions about the growth and direction of the business.

In addition to internal users, external users such as creditors and potential investors may also be interested in the accounting information of the partnership. Creditors may examine the partnership's financial statements to assess the risk associated with lending to the partnership, while potential investors may use the information to decide if they will invest in the business.

Overall, accurate and reliable accounting information is crucial for the success of a partnership like Smith & Jones. It helps the partners monitor the financial health of the business, make informed decisions, and comply with tax reporting requirements. It also helps external users make informed decisions about lending to or investing in the partnership.
