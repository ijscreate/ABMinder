Computation of Profit and Loss

The Profit and Loss formula is used in mathematics to determine the price of a commodity in the market and understand how profitable a business is. Every product has a cost price and a selling price. Based on the values of these prices, we can calculate the profit gained or the loss incurred for a particular product. The important terms covered here are cost price, fixed, variable and semi-variable cost, selling price, marked price, list price, margin, etc. Also, we will learn the profit and loss percentage formula here.

  • Profit: Occurs when the Selling Price is higher than the Cost Price, resulting in a positive gain.
  • Loss: Occurs when the Selling Price is lower than the Cost Price, leading to a negative outcome.
  • Profit Percentage: Reflects the relative increase from the Cost Price to the Selling Price.
  • Loss Percentage: Represents the relative decrease from the Cost Price to the Selling Price.
  • Cost Price (CP): The initial price of a product before any additional expenses or profit.
  • Selling Price (SP): The price at which a product is sold to customers.
  • Marked Price: The original price of a product before any discounts or promotions.
  • List Price: The price set by the seller as the initial value of the product.
  • Margin: The difference between the Cost Price and the Selling Price, representing the profit.

    • Example 1:

    • Cost Price (CP): ₱50
    • Selling Price (SP): ₱70

    Profit: ₱70 - ₱50 = ₱20

    Profit Percentage: (₱20 / ₱50) * 100 = 40%

    Result: The profit made in this transaction is ₱20, and the profit percentage is 40%.

    Example 2:

    • Cost Price (CP): ₱80
    • Selling Price (SP): ₱60

    Loss: ₱80 - ₱60 = ₱20

    Loss Percentage: (₱20 / ₱80) * 100 = 25%

    Result: The loss incurred in this transaction is ₱20, and the loss percentage is 25%.

    Formulas:

    1. Profit:

    It represents the positive difference between the Selling Price (SP) and the Cost Price (CP).

    Formula: Profit = Selling Price - Cost Price

    2. Loss:

    It represents the negative difference between the Selling Price (SP) and the Cost Price (CP).

    Formula: Loss = Cost Price - Selling Price

    3. Profit Percentage:

    It indicates the percentage increase from the Cost Price to the Selling Price.

    Formula: Profit Percentage = (Profit / Cost Price) * 100

    4. Loss Percentage:

    It represents the percentage decrease from the Cost Price to the Selling Price.

    Formula: Loss Percentage = (Loss / Cost Price) * 100

    5. Selling Price

    It is the price at which the commodity is sold per unit.

    Formula: Selling Price = Cost Price + Operating Cost + Profit

    Real-world Applications:

    Scenario 1:

    A business buys a product for ₱150 and sells it for ₱200. 

    Profit:
            ₱200 - ₱150 = ₱50
Profit Percentage:
            (₱50 / ₱150) * 100 ≈ 33.33%

    Decision-Making: Understanding the profit percentage helps the business assess the profitability of the transaction.

    Scenario 2:

    A clothing store buys a shirt for ₱150 and sells it for ₱120 during a clearance sale.

    Loss:
            ₱150 - ₱120 = ₱30
Loss Percentage:
            (₱30 / ₱150) * 100 = 20%

    Decision-Making: Analyzing the loss percentage helps the store evaluate the impact of the clearance sale on the shirt.

    Scenario 3:

    A tech company manufactures a gadget with a production cost of ₱500 and sells it for ₱600. 

    Profit:
            ₱600 - ₱500 = ₱100
Profit Percentage:
            (₱100 / ₱500) * 100 = 20%

    Decision-Making: Analyzing the profit percentage aids the tech company in assessing the success of the gadget launch.

    Scenario 4:

    A farmer invests ₱200 in growing crops but faces a bad harvest, resulting in a selling price of ₱100 for the produce. 

    Loss:
            ₱200 - ₱100 = ₱100
Loss Percentage:
            (₱100 / ₱200) * 100 = 50%
    Result: The farmer incurred a loss of ₱100, and the loss percentage is 50%.

    Scenario 5:

    A bookstore purchases a set of books for ₱300 and sells them for ₱400. 

    Profit:
            ₱400 - ₱300 = ₱100
Profit Percentage:
            (₱100 / ₱300) * 100 ≈ 33.33%
    Decision-Making: The profit percentage helps the bookstore assess the success of selling the book set.
