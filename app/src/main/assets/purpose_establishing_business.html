The Purpose of Establishing Business Enterprises

Introduction: The purpose of establishing a business enterprise has been a subject of intense debate and varying perspectives. Economists, philosophers, business leaders, and scholars have grappled with the question of whether profit maximization should be the sole objective or if businesses should prioritize the interests of various stakeholders and society at large. This lesson delves into the contrasting views and arguments surrounding this complex issue.

I. Profit Maximization

A. Definition: According to economist Milton Friedman and his influential doctrine, the primary purpose of a business is to maximize profits for its owners, particularly the stockholders in the case of publicly-traded companies.

B. Rationale:

  • Profit as a measure of efficiency and resource allocation: Friedman argued that businesses that maximize profits are effectively using resources efficiently, leading to optimal resource allocation in the economy.
  • Aligning the interests of owners and management: By focusing on profit maximization, the interests of the owners (shareholders) and the management are aligned, reducing potential conflicts of interest.
  • Enabling reinvestment and growth: Profitable businesses can reinvest their earnings to fuel growth, innovation, and job creation, ultimately benefiting society.

C. Counterarguments:

  • Neglecting stakeholder interests: Critics argue that solely prioritizing profits can lead to the exploitation of employees, disregard for customer needs, and environmental degradation.
  • Short-term focus: The emphasis on maximizing short-term profits may undermine long-term sustainability and societal well-being.
  • Ethical concerns: Some argue that profit maximization at all costs can lead to unethical business practices and a disregard for moral considerations.

II. Stakeholder Theory

A. Definition: This perspective contends that a business's principal purpose is to serve the interests of a broader group of stakeholders, including employees, customers, society, and the environment, in addition to shareholders.

B. Arguments:

  • Sustainable financial returns require considering stakeholder aspirations: Proponents argue that sustainable profits are achievable only by considering and addressing the legitimate interests and concerns of various stakeholders.
  • Balancing the interests of affected parties, even when conflicting: Businesses operate within a complex web of stakeholder relationships, and managing these interests, even when they conflict, is crucial for long-term success.
  • Corporate social responsibility (CSR) and the shared ecosystem concept: This conception highlights that businesses, consumers, and society are part of a shared ecosystem, and the long-term health of this ecosystem must be maintained above all else.

  • Bill Clinton's view on prioritizing customers and employees over shareholders: Former US President Bill Clinton argued that major multinational companies must put the interests of their customers and employees before those of shareholders to promote economic development and growth, especially in emerging markets.

III. Social Benefit

A. Economic Value-Added: Proponents of this view argue that concepts such as "economic value-added" are useful in balancing profit-making objectives with other ends, such as societal well-being and environmental sustainability.

B. Creating shared value: Businesses can create shared value by addressing societal needs and challenges, thereby generating economic value while simultaneously creating value for society.

C. Examples:

  • Unilever's Sustainable Living Plan: The consumer goods company Unilever has implemented a Sustainable Living Plan that aims to decouple its growth from environmental impact, while increasing its positive social impact.
  • Patagonia's environmental activism: The outdoor clothing company Patagonia is known for its commitment to environmental causes, donating a portion of its profits to environmental organizations and advocating for sustainable business practices.

IV. Innovation as a Goal

A. Creative Expansion: Rohit Kishore, an innovation expert, persuades that business can also be viewed to exist for creative expansion, where successful firms like Google manage to align their activities towards the purpose of creative expansion from the perspective of all stakeholders, especially employees.

B. Innovation as a core principle for corporate survival and success: In today's rapidly changing business landscape, innovation is increasingly recognized as a critical factor for long-term success and competitiveness.

C. Examples:

  • Google's focus on innovation and employee empowerment: Google is renowned for its innovative culture, empowering employees to dedicate a portion of their time to personal projects, leading to groundbreaking innovations.
  • Apple's design-driven innovation: Apple's success is often attributed to its focus on design-driven innovation, creating products that seamlessly blend functionality and aesthetics, captivating consumers.

V. Contract Theory

A. Definition: Advocates of business contract theory believe that a business is a community of participants organized around a common purpose, with legitimate interests and rights over its affairs.

B. Representative Democracy: The enterprise is run by employees and managers as representatives of the participants, akin to a representative democracy.

C. Stakeholder participation: Contract theorists argue that stakeholders, such as employees, customers, and suppliers, have a legitimate interest in how the business is conducted and should have a voice in decision-making processes.

VI. Stakeholder Theory (Expanded)

A. Definition: Stakeholder theorists take contract theory a step further, maintaining that people outside the business enterprise, such as consumers, community members affected by its operations, and broader society, should also have a say in how the business is run.

B. Addressing societal impacts: Businesses have a responsibility to consider and address the societal impacts of their operations, including environmental, social, and economic factors.

C. Multi-stakeholder dialogue and collaboration: Stakeholder theorists advocate for open dialogue and collaboration between businesses, stakeholders, and society to address complex challenges and foster sustainable practices.

VII. Business as Property

A. Definition: This view holds that a business is essentially someone's private property, and the owners have the right to dispose of it as they see fit, within legal and moral boundaries.

B. Arguments:

  • Workers exchange labor for wages and have no inherent right to govern the business: Proponents argue that workers voluntarily exchange their labor for wages from the business owner and have no inherent right to dictate how the owner manages their property.
  • Consumers have no inherent rights over the business, assuming honest and transparent business practices: Assuming the business has provided goods and services honestly and with full disclosure, consumers have no inherent rights to govern the business, which belongs to someone else.
  • Owners' rights are constrained by morality and the law: While acknowledging property rights, this view recognizes that owners' rights are constrained by moral considerations and legal regulations, such as environmental laws and worker safety standards.

C. Counterarguments:

  • Neglecting stakeholder interests and societal impacts: Critics argue that this view fails to account for the legitimate interests of stakeholders and the broader societal impacts of business activities.
  • Short-term focus and externalities: There is a concern that a narrow focus on property rights may lead to short-term decision-making and the neglect of long-term externalities, such as environmental degradation.

The purpose of establishing a business enterprise is a complex and multifaceted issue, with various perspectives emphasizing profit maximization, stakeholder interests, social benefit, innovation, contract theory, and property rights. Each view offers unique insights and considerations, reflecting the intricate web of relationships and impacts that businesses have on various stakeholders and society as a whole.

Ultimately, the purpose of a business enterprise may involve striking a delicate balance between these different considerations, guided by ethical principles, a commitment to sustainable and responsible business practices, and a recognition of the interconnectedness between businesses, stakeholders, and the broader ecosystem in which they operate.

As the business landscape continues to evolve, with increasing emphasis on corporate social responsibility, environmental sustainability, and stakeholder engagement, businesses may need to adopt a more holistic approach that harmonizes economic goals with societal and environmental concerns. Effective stakeholder dialogue, transparency, and a willingness to adapt to changing societal expectations will be crucial for businesses to navigate this complex terrain and redefine their purpose in a way that creates value for all stakeholders while contributing to a more sustainable and equitable world.
