Simple Interest

What is Simple Interest?

Simple interest is a straightforward method of calculating interest on a loan or investment. It is based on the original principal amount (the initial sum of money) and the predetermined interest rate.

Simple interest is an interest charge that ​borrowers pay ​lenders for a loan, calculated based solely on the principal amount without compounding interest. The formula for simple interest is straightforward, and it is commonly used in ​automobile loans or short-term personal loans. Simple interest does not involve compounding, making it easier for borrowers to understand and potentially resulting in lower borrowing costs compared to ​compound interest.

The formula for simple interest is:

I = P R T I = P R T

Finding the Principal Amount (P): If you know the interest, rate, and time, you can find the principal amount using the formula:

P = I r × t P = \frac{I}{r \times t}

Finding the Rate of Interest: If you know the interest, principal, and time, you can find the rate of interest using the formula:

r = I P × t r = \frac{I}{P \times t}
  • The result will be in decimal form. Multiply by 100 to convert it to a percentage.

Finding the Time Period (t): If you know the interest, principal, and rate, you can find the time using the formula:

t = I P × r t = \frac{I}{P \times r}

Where:

  • I is the interest
  • P is the principal amount (the initial amount of money)
  • R is the annual interest rate (expressed as a decimal)
  • T is the time the money is borrowed or invested for, usually measured in years

When calculating simple interest for periods less than a year, such as months or days, you can use adjusted formulas to account for the fractional time periods.

For calculating simple interest for months, the formula is:

I = P R ( T 12 ) I = PR\left(\frac{T}{365}\right)

Where:

  • I is the interest
  • P is the principal amount (the initial amount of money)
  • R is the annual interest rate (in decimal form)
  • T is the time the money is invested or borrowed for (in months)

Similarly, for calculating simple interest for days, the formula is:

I = P R ( T 365 ) I = PR\left(\frac{T}{365}\right)

Where:

  • I is the interest
  • P is the principal amount (the initial amount of money)
  • R is the annual interest rate (in decimal form)
  • T is the time the money is invested or borrowed for (in days)

    • Note that these formulas assume that the interest rate is an annual rate. If the interest rate is given as a monthly or daily rate, you would need to adjust the formulas accordingly.

    Important! The time must be in years to apply the simple interest formula. If you are given months, use a fraction to represent it as years.

    Another type of problem you might run into when working with simple interest is finding the total amount owed or the total value of an investment after a given amount of time. This is known as the future value, and can be calculated in a couple of different ways.

    The future value in simple interest refers to the total amount of money accumulated or owed after a certain period of time when simple interest is applied to the principal amount. It represents the sum of the principal amount and the interest earned or charged over the specified time period.

    Future value can be calculated using the simple interest formula:

    F V = P + S I FV = P + SI

    Alternatively, we can express the formula as:

    F V = P V × ( 1 + r t ) FV = PV \times (1 + rt)

    where:

    • FV is the future value of the investment/loan, including interest
    • PV is the principal investment amount (the initial deposit or loan amount)
    • SI is the simple interest
    • r is the annual interest rate (in decimal)
    • t is the number of years the money is invested or borrowed for

Real-World Examples:

Example 1: Paying Back a Personal Loan

Imagine you took out a personal loan of $2000 from a bank at an annual interest rate of 8% for 2 years to purchase a new laptop. Using the simple interest formula:

Principal (P) = $2000
Interest Rate (R) = 8% or 0.08
Time (T) = 2 years
Simple Interest (SI) = P × R × T = $2000 × 0.08 × 2 = $320

The total amount to be repaid would be:

Total Amount = Principal + Simple Interest = $2000 + $320 = $2320

So, you would need to repay a total of $2320 to the bank over the 2-year period.

Example 2: Earning Interest on a Savings Account

Suppose you deposit $5000 into a savings account with an annual interest rate of 4% offered by your bank. After 5 years, using simple interest:

Principal (P) = $5000
Interest Rate (R) = 4% or 0.04
Time (T) = 5 years
Simple Interest (SI) = P × R × T = $5000 × 0.04 × 5 = $1000

The future value of your savings account after 5 years would be:

Total Savings = Principal + Simple Interest = $5000 + $1000 = $6000

So, your savings account would have a total of $6000 after 5 years, including the interest earned.

Pros of Simple Interest:

  • Easy to Understand: Simple interest calculations are straightforward and easy to comprehend, making it accessible to individuals without advanced financial knowledge.
  • Simple Calculation: The calculation of simple interest involves multiplying the principal amount, interest rate, and time period, which can be done quickly without the need for complex formulas or calculations.
  • Transparent: Since simple interest does not involve compounding, the total interest payable remains constant throughout the loan or investment term, providing clarity to borrowers or investors.
  • Useful for Short-term Loans: Simple interest is well-suited for short-term loans or investments where the interest is calculated periodically based on the original principal amount.

Cons of Simple Interest:

  • Higher Total Interest Paid: Compared to compound interest, simple interest typically results in higher total interest paid over the life of a loan or investment, especially for longer terms.
  • Not Reflective of Changing Balances: Simple interest does not account for changes in the principal amount over time, which may result in overestimation or underestimation of the true interest cost or earnings.
  • Limited Growth Potential: Investments using simple interest may experience slower growth compared to those utilizing compound interest, as the interest earned does not compound over time.
  • Less Flexible: Simple interest may not be as flexible or adaptable to changing financial circumstances or fluctuations in interest rates compared to more complex interest calculation methods.

Key Points:

  • Simple interest is calculated by multiplying the loan principal by the interest rate and the term of the loan.
  • It provides borrowers with a basic idea of borrowing costs and is commonly used in ​auto loans and short-term personal loans.
  • Simple interest does not involve compounding of interest.
  • Benefits of simple interest include lower borrowing costs, easier debt repayment, and a straightforward calculation method.
  • In comparison to compound interest, simple interest does not result in interest being added to the principal amount.
  • Simple interest is advantageous for borrowers, while compound interest is vital for building wealth for investors.
  • Simple interest instruments like most personal loans and coupon-paying bonds provide a straightforward calculation method.
  • Compound interest instruments like bank deposit accounts and credit cards involve interest-on-interest and can lead to higher total payments.
