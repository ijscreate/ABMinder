Working Capital

Working capital is a measure of a company's liquidity and operational efficiency, calculated as the difference between its current assets and current liabilities.

The formula for working capital is:

Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities \text{Working Capital} = \text{Current Assets} - \text{Current Liabilities}
  • Current Assets: These are resources that a company expects to convert to cash within one fiscal year or operating cycle, whichever is longer. Current assets include cash, accounts receivable, inventory, marketable securities, prepaid expenses, and other liquid assets.
  • Current Liabilities: These are obligations that a company must pay within one fiscal year or operating cycle, whichever is longer. Current liabilities include accounts payable, accrued liabilities, short-term debt, and the current portion of long-term debt.

A positive working capital indicates that a company has enough assets to cover its short-term liabilities, while a negative working capital suggests the opposite. However, the optimal level of working capital depends on the industry, the particular company, and its specific circumstances. It’s always a good idea to compare a company’s working capital with that of other companies in the same industry.

Working capital is important because it indicates a company's ability to meet its short-term financial obligations and fund its day-to-day operations. A positive working capital balance indicates that a company has enough short-term assets to cover its short-term liabilities, while a negative working capital balance indicates that a company may have difficulty meeting its short-term obligations.

Common Uses of Working Capital

  • Inventory management: Working capital can be used to purchase inventory and raw materials, ensuring that a business has enough stock on hand to meet customer demand.
  • Accounts receivable: Working capital can be used to finance accounts receivable, allowing a business to extend credit to customers and improve sales.
  • Operating expenses: Working capital can be used to cover operating expenses, such as salaries, rent, and utilities, ensuring that a business can continue to operate smoothly.
  • Short-term financing: Working capital can be used as a source of short-term financing, allowing a business to take advantage of opportunities as they arise, such as purchasing equipment or inventory at a discount.
  • Cash flow management: Working capital can be used to manage cash flow, ensuring that a business has enough liquidity to meet its short-term obligations and avoid cash flow problems.
  • Debt management: Working capital can be used to pay down debt, reducing interest expenses and improving a business's financial position.

Example of Working Capital Calculation

Current Assets:

  • Cash and cash equivalents: PHP 1,500,000
  • Accounts receivable: PHP 2,000,000
  • Inventory: PHP 5,000,000

Total current assets: PHP 8,500,000

Current Liabilities:

  • Accounts payable: PHP 1,000,000
  • Accrued expenses: PHP 500,000
  • Short-term debt: PHP 2,000,000

Total current liabilities: PHP 3,500,000

Working Capital Calculation:

Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

Working Capital = PHP 8,500,000 - PHP 3,500,000

Working Capital = PHP 5,000,000

In this example, the manufacturing company has a working capital of PHP 5,000,000, which means it has enough current assets to meet its short-term obligations of PHP 3,500,000 and still have PHP 1,500,000 left for other operational needs. This is a positive sign of the company's financial health, as it indicates that the company can meet its short-term obligations and still have enough liquidity to take advantage of growth opportunities.
